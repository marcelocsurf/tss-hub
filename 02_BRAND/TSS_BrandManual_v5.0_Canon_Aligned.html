chapter 01

Brand Overview

TSS is a licensed educational methodology — the most systematically structured approach to surf coaching in the world.

Positioning

TSS operates in the same category as PADI (diving certification), CrossFit (fitness methodology), and Gracie Jiu-Jitsu (martial arts). We are an educational system first, not a lifestyle brand, not a franchise, and not a personal brand.

What TSS IS

  • Licensed Educational Methodology — Proprietary, science-based system for structured surf education
  • Certification System — For coaches to achieve recognized, portable credentials
  • Progressive Belt System — Gamified progression from White to Black Belt
  • Scalable Global System — Academy licensing model enabling global adoption
  • Operational Framework — Complete system backed by digital platform (TSS Brain)

What TSS IS NOT

  • A surf school or camp
  • A lifestyle brand
  • A standalone mobile app
  • A franchise
  • A personal brand

The Category We Define

Surfing needed what other sports already had: a systematic, internationally recognized, certification-backed methodology for structured learning and instructor training. TSS fills that gap. With surfing's Olympic inclusion in 2021, the need for educational infrastructure became urgent—and surfing's infrastructure remains decades behind comparable sports. TSS is that infrastructure.

System Architecture: Five Components

1. TSS Methodology

Science-based instructional framework built on Fibonacci principles, progressive sequencing, and the Four Pillars.

2. Belt Progression

Gamified 6-level system (White → Black) providing clear developmental milestones and visual identity.

3. Coach Certification

5-tier credential system (L1–L5) ensuring teaching quality and portability across academies.

4. Academy Licensing

Global rollout model enabling established schools to adopt TSS curriculum and brand.

5. TSS Brain

Digital platform managing coach credentials, student progress, lesson planning, and reporting.

chapter 10

Methodology: The Method

The scientific foundation of TSS. All instruction, progression, and coaching decisions stem from these core principles.

10.1 P·R·C·H — The Four Basic Movements

Every maneuver in surfing is built from four fundamental movements. These are the building blocks of all intermediate and advanced techniques.

P — Posture

Position: Straight back, head up, eyes focused forward.

Alignment: Body centered over board. Shoulders and hips aligned vertically.

Purpose: Foundation for all movement. Without posture, rotation and compression fail.

R — Rotation

Sequence: Eyes → neck → torso → obliques → hips → ankles.

Motor: The engine of every turn. Drives power from upper body through feet to board.

Range: Full-body engagement. Cannot be isolated to hips or shoulders alone.

C — Compression

Action: Lower center of gravity by bending knees and ankles.

Effect: Prepares body for explosive movement. Absorbs wave energy. Builds speed.

Application: Used before turns, landings, and power generation moments.

H — Hold

Technique: Maintain position longer than feels natural. Hold through apex of maneuver before releasing.

Result: Maximum pressure on wave face. Cleanest execution. Controlled exit.

Principle: Patience creates power. Hold = commitment.

10.2 Three Circles of Power

A diagnostic framework for understanding what influences surfing performance. Used by coaches to diagnose problems and plan progression.

The Three Circles (Surfer Version)

Circle 1: Body (P·R·C·H)

What the surfer's body does—posture, rotation, compression, hold. The technical foundation.

Circle 2: Feet Position on Board

Where the surfer stands—tail, neutral, forward. Determines leverage and control.

Circle 3: Wave Dynamics

External environment—slope, speed, pocket, face angle. What the wave provides to work with.

Intersection = Surfing Outcome
Performance is the sum of all three circles. Weak technique (Circle 1) can't be saved by perfect positioning (Circle 2) or ideal waves (Circle 3).

The Three Circles (Coach Version)

Circle 1: Student Level + Requirements

Belt level, prerequisite skills, current gap. What the student can and can't do.

Circle 2: Environment/Conditions

Beach, swell, wind, crowd, time of day. What's available to practice in.

Circle 3: Task or Game

The specific exercise or progression step. How the coach structures the learning moment.

Intersection = Learning Design
Effective coaching aligns all three. Wrong task (Circle 3) for the student's level (Circle 1) in bad conditions (Circle 2) = wasted session.

10.3 Foot Positions (FP1, FP2, FP3)

FP1 — Full Tail

Position: Back foot at extreme tail of board.

Leverage: Maximum. Entire board becomes a lever arm.

Use: Tight turns, sharp direction changes, launching aerials.

Stability: Lower. Requires core strength and balance.

FP2 — Neutral

Position: Back foot centered over middle third of board.

Leverage: Balanced. Equal pressure forward and aft.

Use: Default riding position. Smooth carving. Flow state.

Stability: Highest. Most natural, least energy expenditure.

FP3 — Forward

Position: Back foot moved forward (toward center, away from tail).

Leverage: Reduced on tail. Increases nose control.

Use: Maximum speed generation. Straight-line paddling. Small wave surfing.

Stability: Very high. Low center of gravity.

10.4 Four Pillars Framework

All TSS instruction is organized around four interdependent pillars. Each must develop in parallel. Safety is a transversal layer that spans all four.

Physical

Strength, endurance, flexibility, body awareness. The physical capacity to execute techniques.

Technical

P·R·C·H, foot positions, sequences. The mechanics of surfing maneuvers.

Tactical

Wave reading, position selection, decision-making. How to choose the right move at the right moment.

Mental

Focus, resilience, confidence, learning mindset. The psychological foundation of consistent improvement.

Safety as Transversal Layer

Safety awareness is woven throughout all four pillars and all progression. A student cannot advance to the next belt without demonstrating safe practices: ocean awareness, board handling, peer responsibility, self-awareness of limits.

10.5 TSS Execution Sequences

Detailed step-by-step maneuver progressions. Sequences are belt-specific and built using blocks (detailed in Chapter 11).

Frontside Sequence

  1. Posture: Establish upright, centered position on the wave face
  2. Bottom Turn: Compress and rotate to change direction at wave base
  3. Projection: Extend up the wave face with momentum
  4. Cruz (Avioncito): Smooth, arcing turn on the upper shoulder
  5. Variation: Add cutback, hack, or radical maneuver specific to belt level
  6. Granada: Return to lower face for next opportunity
  7. Return to Posture: Reset position and repeat or exit wave

Backside Sequence

  1. Posture: Establish upright, centered position (back to wave face)
  2. Bottom Turn: Compress and rotate for direction change and projection
  3. Choke: Tight, controlled turn keeping upper body compact
  4. Tapaloco: Quick pivoting turn in middle section
  5. Elbow: Extension into aerial or advanced maneuver
  6. Variation: Adapt to wave pocket and available slope
  7. Touch the Board: Intentional board contact for control and show
  8. Return to Posture: Reset and prepare for next section

Phase Equivalence Table

Phase Frontside Sequence Backside Sequence
1 Posture Posture
2 Bottom Turn Bottom Turn
3 Projection Choke
4 Cruz Tapaloco
5 Variation Elbow / Variation
6 Granada Touch the Board
7 Return to Posture Return to Posture
chapter 11

Block System & Infinite Circle

11.1 The Eight Learning Blocks (Blocks 0–7)

Blocks are functional learning units. Each block isolates a specific skill or concept for focused practice. Blocks are combined into sequences and used across all belt levels.

Block 0: Awareness

Ocean safety, board handling, wave dynamics observation. Foundation for all subsequent learning. Ongoing throughout progression.

Block 1: Entry

Paddling, wave selection, positioning, pop-up. Getting into waves consistently.

Block 2: Foundation

P·R·C·H basics, balance, board control. Establishing technical fundamentals.

Block 3: Direction Change

Bottom turns, basic turning mechanics. First maneuver mastery.

Block 4: Carving

Smooth, flowing turns. Linking multiple maneuvers. Style and efficiency.

Block 5: Projection

Speed generation, vertical movement, wave face usage. Power surfing entry.

Block 6: Variation

Maneuver creativity, adaptation, situational awareness. Beyond basics.

Block 7: Integration

Full-wave expression, combining all blocks, competition ready. Mastery.

11.2 Architectural Rule: Sequential & Cyclical Progression

Blocks 1–3: Sequential

Must be mastered in order. Block 1 (Entry) is prerequisite for Block 2 (Foundation). Block 2 is prerequisite for Block 3 (Direction Change). Cannot skip or reverse.

Blocks 4–7: Cyclical

After Block 3 mastery, blocks 4–7 loop. Students cycle through Carving → Projection → Variation → Integration → back to Carving at deeper levels. Each cycle adds complexity and refinement.

11.3 The Infinite Circle Concept

Once a surfer reaches Block 4 (Carving), learning becomes cyclical rather than linear. The same four blocks (Carving, Projection, Variation, Integration) repeat at progressively deeper, more nuanced levels.

Linear Progression: Blocks 1–3

Entry → Foundation → Direction Change → (Ready for Cycle)

Infinite Cycle: Blocks 4–7

Carving ⟲ Projection ⟲ Variation ⟲ Integration ⟲ (repeat with deeper mastery)

This cycle continues through Purple, Brown, and Black Belt. Each rotation adds precision, power, and creative expression.

11.4 Entry Sequence: The Sweet Spot Process

The foundation for all Block-based learning. Students master this before progressing into block cycles.

  1. Sweet Spot Identification: Find the peak of the forming wave
  2. Chase Wave: Paddle to position ahead of the breaking section
  3. Paddling Angle: Angle board at 45° to incoming wave
  4. Cobra: Extend arms (cobra position) for final paddle strokes
  5. Pop-up: Explosive movement from prone to standing position

11.5 Doctrinal Rule: Blocks Don't Replace Sequences

Critical Distinction

Blocks = Skill Building Units
Isolated practice of specific skills (bottom turn practice, carving practice, etc.)

Sequences = Integrated Movement Progressions
Complete wave-riding from entry through exit (e.g., Frontside Sequence in Chapter 10)

Relationship: Sequences are built USING blocks. A Blue Belt Frontside Sequence might emphasize Block 2 (Foundation) + Block 3 (Direction Change), while a Brown Belt sequence adds Block 5 (Projection) + Block 6 (Variation).

11.6 Relationship Map: Four Core Frameworks

Three Circles of Power

Language for diagnosis. How do we talk about what we observe? (Body, Board Position, Wave Dynamics)

Blocks (0–7)

Functional architecture. What are the learning components? (Entry, Foundation, Direction Change, etc.)

Infinite Circle (Blocks 4–7 Cycle)

Operational loop. How does learning progress after fundamentals? (Recurring Carving → Projection → Variation → Integration)

Belt System

Developmental stage. What level of complexity is the student ready for? (White–Black)

Sequences

Stage-specific progression. What integrated movement should the student practice? (Frontside/Backside Sequences per belt)

Renewable

Certifications expire annually. Renewal requires continuing education, student feedback, and annual assessment. Keeps coaches current.

Progressive

Coaches advance level-to-level. Each level requires higher belt status and additional experience. Ensures quality at every level.

12.4 Authority Structure: TSS Global (Enkrateia)

TSS Global (Enkrateia) is the sole authority for all coach certifications.

No individual coach, academy, or regional body can independently certify coaches. All L1–L5 certifications are issued by and registered with TSS Global. This ensures:

  • Global consistency in teaching quality
  • Portable credentials across all academies
  • Auditable credential verification
  • Methodology integrity and evolution management

12.6 Annual Renewal Requirements

All active certifications must renew annually to remain valid.

Continuing Education

Minimum 20 hours annual training. Can include TSS workshops, peer learning, external coaching education, or advanced study.

Student Feedback

Minimum 3 student evaluations per year. Coaches must maintain 4.0+ average (out of 5). Low scores trigger support plan.

Annual Assessment

Brief reassessment confirming continued competency at current level. Written component + teaching demonstration.

Integrity Check
