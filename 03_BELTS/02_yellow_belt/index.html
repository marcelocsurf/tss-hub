Welcome to Yellow Belt

You have completed your White Belt foundation. You can control your board, find your sweet spot, pop up, hold a power stance, and ride whitewater with intention. Now everything changes.

The Yellow Belt is your transition from whitewater to green waves — unbroken waves with real structure, real energy, and real decisions. This is the most significant shift in your entire surfing journey. You will learn to read the wave before it breaks, choose where to position yourself, manage your paddling speed, and angle your takeoff in the direction the wave is moving.

The Core Shift at Yellow Belt

At White Belt, the wave came to the surfer. At Yellow Belt, the surfer goes to the wave.

This is not a bigger version of White Belt. The environment changes from whitewater to green waves, and that changes everything: what the surfer sees, how they decide, how they paddle, and how they relate to the wave's energy. The transformation is from reactive surfing to intentional surfing.

This belt builds three things:

  • Wave Relationship — Reading structure, energy, and the pocket
  • Decision-Making Under Pressure — Which wave, which direction, when to commit
  • Functional Independence — Managing a full session with decreasing coach direction
Foundation — Before We Begin

ONE WAVE — Learning to Learn

Before you can improve your surfing, you need to prepare the tool you use to learn: your mind. This section is based on the book ONE WAVE by Marcelo Castellanos.

ONE WAVE
The Surfer's Guide to Focus, Flow and Conscious Progress

Nobody teaches surfers how to train. There are countless resources on technique, but what doesn't exist is a guide to the mental architecture of learning itself. How to focus. How to design a session with intention. How to move through frustration without letting it derail you. How to build the internal conditions that allow real progress to happen.

The Core Principle: One Wave, One Intention

The brain learns best when it has a single, clear focus. Not five objectives. Not a general intention to surf well. One thing. One wave. One specific intention carried into the water with full attention.

Free surfing and training are not the same activity. Free surfing is expression — using the tools you already have. Training is intervention — deliberately introducing something new with the specific intention of building a capability that doesn't fully exist yet.

Be Here and Now — The Art of Focus

The mind escapes in two directions: into the future (anxiety about performance, worry about results) or into the past (replaying mistakes, comparing to previous sessions). The only moment where learning happens is now. This wave. This feedback from the board under your feet.

When attention centers, noise fades. When attention divides, noise returns.

Mental Tool — Take Out the Trash

"Taking out the trash" means deliberately clearing mental clutter before you paddle out. Not suppressing it — acknowledging it and choosing to set it down. The ocean doesn't care about your day. But you can choose what you bring to it.

Mental Tool — 30-Second Pre-Session Ritual

Before every session:

Sit or stand still for 30 seconds. Ask: Where am I? (Here.) What time is it? (Now.) Take three slow, deliberate breaths. Then choose one focus for this session — not a goal, a focus. Something you will pay attention to while you surf.

Mental Tool — Curiosity Over Performance

The surfer thinking "I need to land this" is performing. The surfer thinking "I wonder what happens when I try this" is learning. Curiosity opens the nervous system. Dopamine releases. Pattern recognition improves. Enter the water with curiosity, not desperation.

The Comfort Zone & Why Growth Feels Like Regression

When you introduce a new skill, the nervous system processes it consciously — slowly, effortfully. Everything else gets slightly worse. The timing breaks. The reads slow. This is not getting worse. It is evidence of change.

"The price of evolution is temporarily abandoning your comfort zone with the certainty that the regression you feel is part of the process."

Mental Tool — Frustration is Information

Frustration is not a verdict on your ability. It is information that the nervous system is being asked to do something it cannot yet do automatically. That gap — between where you are and what you're attempting — is exactly where growth happens. The surfer who never feels frustrated is not pushing beyond the automatic.

Mental Tool — Expect Nothing. Enjoy Everything.

The belief that surfing should feel good all the time is the expectation that causes the most damage. When a session feels off — when the gap between expectation and reality tightens — use this mantra: Expect nothing. Enjoy everything. The ocean doesn't change. You do.

Coach Notes — ONE WAVE Integration

Introduce these concepts during the first theoretical class (Block 0/Block 1). Not as motivation — as operational tools. The 30-second ritual becomes standard pre-session protocol. "One mission today" is the practical application of ONE WAVE's core principle.

When a student shows frustration: do not say "relax." Explain the Flow Channel — they are temporarily in the anxiety zone because the challenge exceeds current capability. Regress the challenge slightly to bring them back into the learning zone.

Block 0 — Wave Literacy

Understanding the Wave

Before riding a green wave, the surfer needs to understand it. This section gives the language and awareness to read the ocean like a surfer, not a swimmer.

Parts of a Wave

  • Face — The clean, unbroken surface. This is where you ride.
  • Pocket — The steepest section where the wave is about to break. Your source of speed and power.
  • Lip — The top edge as it curls over. The impact zone.
  • Shoulder — The softer section ahead of the breaking point. Easier but less powerful.
  • Whitewater — The broken foam. Where you trained at White Belt.
Key Concept

Energy lives in the pocket. Everything depends on your relationship with it. Close to pocket = speed, power, options. Drift to shoulder = slow, weak, stuck.

Types of Waves

  • Spilling — Crumbles gently from the top. Best for learning.
  • Plunging — Lip throws forward with force. More risk.
  • Closeout — Entire wave breaks at once. Avoid — nowhere to go.

Rule: If it looks like a closeout or heavy plunging — do not paddle for it.

Lefts & Rights

Waves break in a direction. A left opens to the left (surfer facing shore). A right opens to the right.

At Yellow Belt, you must identify left or right before you paddle for the wave. This determines your takeoff angle and ride direction.

Wave Stages — The Journey of a Wave

Stage 1: Swell approaches
Stage 2: Face forms
Stage 3: Pocket forms
Stage 4: Breaks to whitewater

You want to catch between Stage 2 and 3 — face formed, pocket building, before it collapses.

Block 0 — Wave Literacy

Safety & Etiquette

Safety is not optional. At Yellow Belt, safety becomes more complex — deeper water, bigger waves, other surfers.

Communication Signals

These must be automatic. Practice until they require zero thinking.

▶
YB-07 / YB-08
Safety at Yellow Belt + Hand Signals Demonstration
3-4 min
  • Coach asks: ARE YOU OK? — You respond with hand signal
  • You are OK: Both arms form a triangle above your head
  • You need help: One arm waving side to side above your head
  • OUT OF THE WATER: Coach crosses both arms in X. Return to shore immediately.

⚠ Safety Rules — Non-Negotiable

  • Never place the surfboard between yourself and an incoming wave
  • Never position yourself in front of another surfer's board
  • When you fall: cover your head with both arms, fall away from the board
  • Never grab the surfboard by the tail or leash when pulled by a wave
  • If in doubt, do not go out. Tell your coach.

Rip Currents

If caught: Do not panic. Do not fight it. Swim parallel to the beach until you exit the current, then swim back at an angle. If you cannot escape: float, conserve energy, signal for help.

Surf Etiquette

  • Priority: Closest to the peak has right of way
  • No drop-in: If someone is riding, do not take their wave
  • No snake: Do not paddle around to steal priority
  • Paddle wide: Go around the breaking zone, not through it
  • Control your board: A loose board is a danger to everyone

Coach Notes — Safety

Conditions limit: Max wave height 2–4 feet green waves. No overhead. No strong currents. If conditions exceed this, drop back to whitewater drills or theory.

Supervision ratio: 1 coach per 4–8 students. Clear visual contact at all times. Coach in water or positioned for immediate water entry.

Critical check: Leash verified every session. Etiquette is not polite theory — it is active safety. Pause session if etiquette is violated.

Block 1 — Preparation

Venue · Goal · Warm-Up

Create awareness, safety, and intention before entering the water.

Step 01 — Venue Analysis

Read the ocean before touching the water. Identify where waves break, which direction, safe zones, hazards, rip currents, rocks, other surfers. At Yellow Belt, do this with minimal coach correction.

Step 02 — Set Goal

Every session needs a clear mission. Not "have fun" — a specific, measurable objective. Example: "Catch 5 green waves with an angled takeoff." You start contributing to goal-setting.

Step 03 — Warm-Up

Activate before entering: mobility, body awareness, mental focus. The warm-up transitions you from daily life into surf mode. Enter the "body zone" where your body is ready to perform.

Coach Delivery — Preparation Block

Venue Analysis: Group exercise using notebook. Students identify safe zone, risks, and spot suitability for level. Coach corrects minimally — the surfer should lead this at Yellow Belt.

Goal Setting: State the mission in one sentence. Explain success criteria. Safety check (leash, signals, safe zone). Surfer restates the mission — if they cannot, simplify.

Warm-Up: Mobility → Body awareness → Mental focus. 5–8 minutes. This is not cardio — it is nervous system activation.

One mission today. What is the mission? Where is the safe zone?
Block 2 — Entry & Control

Board · Paddle · Turtle Roll

Enter the ocean safely and maintain control of your board. This is where Yellow Belt diverges most from White Belt — you are paddling into deeper water through breaking waves.

Steps 04-06: Grab Board → Walk Out → Sweet Spot

Handle the board with intention. Walk to the water safely — board at your side, not in front. Enter and find your sweet spot automatically (this should be automatic from White Belt). The sweet spot is where the board balances perfectly — nose not sinking, not popping up.

Step 07 — Paddle Out

Paddle from shore to the lineup through breaking waves. Use efficient technique: centered position, long deep strokes, elbows high, push water backward not downward. Conserve energy — you need it for catching waves, not just getting out.

  • Board position: Chest slightly lifted, board balanced
  • Stroke: Clean hand entry, long and deep, elbow higher than hand
  • Rhythm: Smooth cadence, then accelerate right before takeoff
  • Core: Engaged for stability. Legs together, relaxed
  • Breathing: Loose shoulders/neck. Controlled breathing to avoid tension

Step 08 — Turtle Roll

When broken whitewater comes toward you, the turtle roll lets you pass through without losing your board.

Read direction
Align nose
Cobra position
Flip (fins up)
Rails + elbows
Stay calm
Punch + kick
Flip back

Coach Notes — Entry & Control

Priority 2 in the teaching stack. The surfer must get from shore to the lineup. If they cannot, nothing else matters.

Common errors:

  • Exhausted before reaching lineup → Not timing lulls, paddling too hard too soon
  • Paddles into impact zone → Goes straight instead of angling around
  • Turtle roll fails → Nose not aligned with incoming foam ("Form the T")
Form the T. Wait for the lull. Paddle wide. Save your energy.
Block 3 — Wave Interaction

Speeds · Pocket · Angle · Cobra

Connect with the wave's energy and position yourself to catch it. This block contains the three new technical elements at Yellow Belt.

Step 09 — Paddling Speeds (1-2-3-4)

Paddling is not one speed. It has gears.

▶
YB-18
Paddling Speeds 1-2-3-4 Demonstration
2 min
Speed 1 — Slow positioning. Moving around the lineup.
Speed 2 — Medium readiness. Set approaching.
Speed 3 — Committed. Wave chosen.
Speed 4 — Full sprint. Maximum power to match the wave.
Key Concept

Paddling is speed management. Think gears in a car. Match intensity to the situation. The right speed at the right moment is more effective than paddling hard all the time.

Step 10 — Chase the Pocket

The pocket is your target. Before the wave arrives, position near where you expect it to form. As the wave approaches, adjust. If the pocket moves, you move. This is active positioning, not passive waiting.

Step 11 — Correct Paddle Angle

At White Belt, you paddled straight toward shore. At Yellow Belt, you angle your board in the direction the wave is breaking. Right-breaking wave = nose right. Left = nose left.

This single adjustment changes everything — instead of racing toward the sand, you ride along the face of the wave.

  • Option 1: Far from the pocket — hard paddle or let go
  • Option 2: Near the pocket — paddle toward it
  • Option 3: In front of the pocket — paddle with its energy
Key Concept — Angle of Approach

Going straight wastes the wave. Angling the takeoff in the wave's direction allows you to ride along the face instead of racing toward shore.

Step 12 — Cobra — Pick Your Line

Once the wave catches you, immediately go into cobra position: chest up, eyes scanning. This is where you choose your line — the direction you will ride. Look where you want to go. The cobra is not just a position. It is a decision moment.

Coach Notes — Wave Interaction (Priority 3 & 4)

This is where Yellow Belt lives. The three new elements — Paddling Speeds, Chase the Pocket, Correct Angle — are the core technical additions.

Most frequent failures:

  • Wrong position relative to pocket → Paddles from shoulder, no energy
  • No angle → Nose at beach, loses wave face
  • Late commitment → Does not shift to Speed 3/4 in time
  • Skips cobra → Straight to pop-up, no line choice
Where is the energy? Get closer. Angle the nose. Speed three. Now four. Three strokes earlier. Cobra first. Choose your line. Commit.
Block 4 — Connect with the Board

Pop-Up · Posture · Impulse

Stand up and establish stability on a moving green wave. The technique from White Belt does not change, but the environment does.

Step 13 — Pop-Up & Foot Position

Your pop-up was trained at White Belt. Now execute it on a moving, curving green wave. Front foot lands center. Back foot over fins. Head up, eyes forward. Lower hips before releasing the board. Precision matters more than speed.

Step 14 — Power Stance

Knees bent, back straight, arms open for balance, weight centered. On a green wave, posture is more demanding. Compression and extension become tools for speed generation, not just stability.

Step 15 — Impulse

Technique for generating or recovering speed. Use after pop-up, after a maneuver, and at closings.

Backside Impulse

Get low → Touch water with both hands → Grab water and push forward → Return to posture

Frontside Impulse

Get low → Touch water with leading hand → Push forward while extending legs → Return to posture

Coach Notes — Connect with Board

Pop-up errors on green waves: Surfer rushes, feet land wrong, hips stay high. Root cause is usually fear of the moving surface.

Correction priority: Two-Second Pop-Up — hands stay on board until eyes are forward and hips are low. One correction per wave maximum.

Eyes forward, hips down. Front foot to the center. Get low. Stay connected.
Block 5 — Flow & Exit

Turns · Up & Down · Dismount

Ride the wave with intention and exit safely. This block includes the new element at Sequence #7.

Step 16 — Turn Left / Right

Use your eyes to lead. Eyes → Head → Torso → Hips → Board. This is the kinetic chain. At Yellow Belt, turns should be intentional and directional, not accidental.

YB-25 / YB-26
Basic Turns (Frontside & Backside) + Go Up and Down the Wave Face
4-5 min
Key Concept — The Kinetic Chain

Sight → Neck & Head Rotation → Torso → Hips → Ankles. Rotation starts with your eyes. If you do not look, you do not turn.

Step 17 — Go Up and Down (Sequence #7)

The new element at Yellow Belt. Ride the wave face vertically — up toward the lip, down toward the trough. Going up generates potential energy. Coming down converts it to speed. This is the physics of wave riding. Stay close to the pocket while moving up and down.

Key Concept — Vertical Movement

Going up = potential energy. Going down = kinetic energy (speed). This is how surfers maintain and generate speed on the wave face.

Step 18 — Starfish Dismount

Every wave ends. Extend arms and legs like a starfish as you fall backward away from the board. This keeps your body flat on the surface. Always cover your head when surfacing.

Coach Notes — Flow & Exit

Go Up and Down scope: The surfer understands the concept and actively attempts it. Consistency and repeatability are Blue Belt criteria. Do not over-correct. Attempting it is the Yellow Belt standard.

Most common ride error: Goes straight to shore, never turns. Root cause: kinetic chain not activating, eyes stay forward instead of leading rotation.

Starfish is non-negotiable. Pause session if skipped. Safety habit must override instinct.

Look where you want to go. Eyes first. Back to the pocket. Starfish. Every time.
Frameworks

Three Circles of Power

The Three Circles are the language we use to understand surfing. Body, Board, Wave. When all three align, you experience Flow. When one breaks, the ride breaks.

Circle 1 — Body: The 4 Basic Movements (P·R·C·H)

These are the four movement patterns your body must master to turn, generate speed, and control the board. They work together as a chain — break one link and the whole system loses power.

P
Posture
The foundation. Back straight, head up, shoulders forward, aligned with the center of the board. Good posture is what makes rotation possible — without it, the kinetic chain has no anchor. Shoulders forward activates the core and enables the obliques to fire during rotation.
R
Rotation
The engine of every turn. Starts with your eyes looking where you want to go → head turns → shoulders and torso open → obliques engage → hips rotate → power transfers to feet → board changes rail. If posture breaks during rotation, power leaks before reaching the board.
C
Compression
Lowers center of gravity to create internal energy. When the wave lacks external energy (power), you create it internally: compress → extend = speed and drive. Like a skateboarder on a half-pipe: compress at the bottom, extend going up.
H
Hold
Sustain the position longer than feels natural. Through a bottom turn, hold the compression+rotation through the apex before releasing. The timing of the hold is what separates clean turns from rushed ones. Hold builds the energy that the release converts to speed.

The Biomechanics of Rotation — Why Posture Matters

Critical teaching point: The rail change (turning) is activated by the obliques. For the obliques to fire effectively, the surfer needs good posture — specifically, shoulders forward and core engaged. When a surfer breaks posture (hunches, looks down, leans back), the obliques cannot engage properly, and the rotation dies at the torso instead of transferring through to the feet and board.

The kinetic chain in sequence: Eyes → Neck → Shoulders → Torso rotation (obliques activate) → Hips → Ankles → Board changes rail

Diagnostic: When a turn has no power — first check: is posture intact through the rotation? Are shoulders forward? Is the front foot centered (axis of rotation)? If the front foot is off-center, the board physically cannot rotate around it.

Shoulders forward! Eyes first, then rotate. Hold it longer.

Circle 2 — Board: Feet Position

Where you place your feet, you place your weight. Weight placement determines how much of the rail engages with the wave, which directly controls your speed and maneuverability.

Position 1 — Full Tail

Rear foot at the very back, over fins / tail pad. Maximum pivot, minimum stability. For tight turns and maneuvers.

Position 2 — Neutral

Rear foot centered, balanced between fins and center. Speed + Stability balanced. Default for wave-face riding.

Position 3 — Forward

Rear foot further forward on the board. Maximum speed, maximum stability, minimum pivot. For generating speed and trimming.

Front foot must always be centered on the stringer. This is the axis of rotation. If the front foot is off-center, the board does not respond to your body's rotation.

Weight further back = less rail engaged = more pivot, less stability. Weight further forward = more rail engaged = more speed, less maneuverability.

Circle 3 — Wave: Dynamic of the Wave

With good body mechanics and correct foot placement, you can now take advantage of the wave's energy. What we are trying to do in surfing is go up and down the wave.

The closer you are to the pocket, the more speed you can achieve. When you have good P·R·C·H and correct feet position, you can go up and down the wave face. The closer your turn is to the breaking part, the more radical your surfing becomes.

Drawing a straight line across the wave face toward the shoulder puts you far from the pocket — riding the weak, slow part. We use turns to stay positioned in the pocket while riding the wave.

The Summation = Flow

Flow is the goal: to move fluently, with speed and power. To look where you want to go and fly there. Flow is what you achieve when all three circles work together. Good body mechanics (P·R·C·H) + correct foot placement + reading and using the wave's energy = Flow.

Three Circles as Diagnostic Tool

When a surfer's execution breaks down, trace it to the circle:

  • Circle 1 (Body): Is the rotation complete? Are the eyes leading? Is compression timed? Is posture collapsing? Are shoulders forward?
  • Circle 2 (Board): Where is the weight? Is the front foot centered? Is the back foot pressuring the correct rail? Are they using a whitewater stance on a green wave?
  • Circle 3 (Wave): Where did they take off relative to the pocket? Was the angle correct? Are they reading the wave direction or guessing?

The block before the failure point is usually where the real problem lives.

The Infinite Circle

Once standing on the wave, Blocks 4–7 recycle in a repeating loop. This is not a separate progression — it is the living execution logic of surfing on a wave.

Entry Sequence (Before the Loop)

Before the Infinite Circle begins, you complete the entry sequence — this happens once per wave:

Why "Infinite"?

This loop can repeat multiple times within a single wave. Each time you return to stable posture, you have the opportunity to rotate again, project again, and execute another action. Surfing is not a single move — it is a repeated architecture with variations.

At Yellow Belt, your goal is not to complete multiple perfect loops. Your goal is to understand: after every action on the wave, return to posture and start again.

The Simple Version

Posture → Rotation → Projection → Maneuver → Recovery → back to Posture. The recovery is what makes the circle infinite. Without recovery to posture, the ride ends at the first action. With recovery, every wave becomes multiple opportunities.

Coach Notes — Infinite Circle as Diagnostic Tool

When a surfer's execution breaks down during a ride, trace the failure to the block:

  • Block 4 problem? — Is posture collapsing? Is the base unstable?
  • Block 5 problem? — Is rotation incomplete? Is the rail not engaging? (Check obliques, shoulders forward)
  • Block 6 problem? — Is there no projection? Is the surfer drifting without direction?
  • Block 7 problem? — Is the maneuver failing because the preceding blocks were incomplete?
  • No return to cycle? — Speed is lost because recovery to posture does not happen

The correct intervention targets the specific block that broke. The block before the failure point is usually where the real problem lives.

At Yellow Belt, Mission 10 (Rotation + Impulse) is the first in-water practice of this concept. The surfer turns, adds impulse to recover, returns to posture, and attempts to go again.

Practice

Missions (Water)

In-water practice assignments. Focus on one mission per session or per set of waves. Missions progress sequentially — each builds on the previous.

How Missions Work

Each mission has a specific objective. Focus on one mission per session or per set of waves. After each attempt, self-assess: Did I attempt the mission? What did I notice? What needs adjustment? Focused practice beats random surfing.

Mission 1

Control My Board

  • Control the board using hands in water
  • Maneuver with intention through whitewater
  • Pass through whitewater maintaining control
  • Perform a successful turtle roll
Key Concept

The board is a tool, not something that drags you.

Mission 2

Sweet Spot + Alignment + Wave Catch

  • Find sweet spot automatically
  • Align with incoming wave
  • Catch with intentional paddling
  • Cobra immediately after catching
  • Turn right or left
  • Controlled starfish dismount
Mission 3

Technical Pop-Up + Power Stance

  • Stable, technical pop-up on green wave
  • Clear power stance immediately
  • Starfish dismount
Key Concept

Stability before speed. When the pop-up is stable, confidence appears naturally.

Mission 4

Front Foot Center

Precise front-foot placement in center. Maintain balance. Repeat across waves.

Mission 5

Brake & Accelerator

Move forward/backward on board. Apply braking and acceleration pressure. Add impulse.

Mission 6

Find the Pocket

  • Seek the pocket before and during the ride
  • Choose best paddling angle based on pocket location
  • Position to catch near the pocket, not from the shoulder
Key Concept

If the pocket moves, you move.

Mission 7

Catch + Cobra + Pick Your Line

  • Catch green wave with committed paddling
  • Cobra immediately
  • Choose line (left or right) and commit
Mission 8

Backside Rotation

Catch → Cobra → Pick backside line → Execute backside rotation → Return to posture

Mission 9

Frontside Rotation

Catch → Cobra → Pick frontside line → Execute frontside rotation → Return to posture

Mission 10

Rotation + Impulse

  • Catch and execute a turn (backside or frontside)
  • Add impulse immediately after
  • Return to posture
  • Attempt a second loop if wave allows
Key Concept

Turn, recover, repeat. Your first taste of the Infinite Circle.

Mission 11

Go Up and Down — Play with the Pocket

  • Surf the face from top to bottom
  • Stay close to the pocket while moving vertically
  • Use gravity (going down) to generate speed
  • Use momentum (going up) to create potential energy

Coach Notes — Mission Delivery

One mission per session. If the surfer needs more than one correction per wave, the session focus is too complex. Simplify.

Mission selection: Follow the sequence. Do not skip to Mission 8 (Backside Rotation) before Mission 6 (Find the Pocket) is functional. Each mission builds on the previous.

Tracking: After each set — surfer reports: Time, Repetitions, Focus (1-10), Difficulty (1-10), Accomplished (yes/no), What to work next. Coach adds one observation.

Wave range: 2–4 feet green waves for all water missions. Drop to whitewater when isolating corrections.

One mission today. Did you attempt the mission? What needs adjustment?
Practice

On-Land Drills

Prepare the nervous system, simplify technique, reduce errors in water, accelerate learning. Practice before every session and between sessions.

Why On-Land Drills Matter

On-land drills prepare your nervous system before entering the water. They simplify technique so your body learns the pattern without the complexity of waves. They reduce errors in the water and accelerate real learning. Today we are not training maneuvers. We are training control, safety, and solid foundations.

Drill L1

Pop-Up + Power Stance (Flat Surface)

Lie flat → Cobra → Pop-up in one movement → Front foot center → Lower hips → Power stance → Hold 2 sec → Eyes forward, back straight. 10 reps. Precision, not speed.

Drill L2

Pop-Up with Transitions & Rotations

Pop-up + hold 2 sec → Position 1: frontside rotation, backside rotation → Position 2: generate momentum → Return Position 1. 5 reps. Fluid movements.

Drill L3

Rotation — Kinetic Chain Activation

Power stance → Look frontside → Let neck rotate, then torso, then hips → Repeat backside. 5 each direction. Start slow, build speed.

Drill L4

Wave Simulation — Top to Bottom Flow

Power stance → Simulate dropping down: compress, shift weight frontside → Simulate going up: extend, shift backside → Fluid transitions rail to rail.

Drill L5

Guided Visualization — Returning to the Pocket

Close eyes → Imagine wave → Visualize pocket → Imagine turn away from pocket → Visualize return → Feel energy increase. 3–5 mental waves.

Drill L6

Bottom Turn Mechanics (No Skateboard)

Power stance → Simulate bottom turn: flexion/extension → Transition to cutback: torso rotation + obliques. 5 backside, 5 frontside. Focus compression at bottom, extension at top.

Coach Notes — On-Land Drill Delivery

Method: Explain → Demonstrate → Participate → Feedback. Coach demonstrates each drill first, then students execute while coach corrects individually.

Focus: Precision over speed. If the pop-up is sloppy on land, it will be worse in the water. Hold students to the standard — 2-second hold, eyes forward, front foot centered.

Duration: 15–20 minutes total for land drills. Do not exhaust students before the water session. These are nervous system activation, not conditioning.

Mind & Emotions

Mental Tools & Flow

Growth requires discomfort. These tools help you breathe inside of it. From the book ONE WAVE by Marcelo Castellanos.

The Flow Channel

Flow lives between anxiety and boredom. When challenge significantly exceeds capability, anxiety shuts you down. When capability far exceeds the challenge, boredom makes you sloppy. The learning zone is just beyond your current automatic capacity — where you miss some attempts and succeed enough to feel the target.

📊
ANXIETY (too hard) ← FLOW CHANNEL → BOREDOM (too easy)
Y-axis: Difficulty/Challenges | X-axis: Time/Skills
The channel moves as you improve. What was challenging at White Belt becomes automatic at Yellow Belt.
Tool 1 — Breath as Navigation

The breath is the only part of the autonomic nervous system you can consciously control. When the mind drifts into anxiety or regret, the breath becomes shallow and fast. Slow the breath deliberately — heart rate lowers, attention narrows, urgency recedes. You cannot control the waves. You can control the breath that determines how you meet them.

Tool 2 — One Wave, One Intention

Each wave in a training session has one objective. Not "surf the best wave of your life" — just execute the specific mission. The ego says: "don't waste a good wave on a drill." Recognize that pattern. The ego protects your self-image at the cost of your growth.

Tool 3 — Frustration is Information

That gap between where you are and what you're attempting — that's where adaptation happens. Without the gap, there is no stimulus for growth. The clumsiness, the awkwardness, the sense of regression — these are evidence of change, not failure.

Tool 4 — Expect Nothing. Enjoy Everything.

The most destructive expectation is that surfing should feel good all the time. Every time the gap between expectation and reality tightens, repeat this mantra. The ocean doesn't change. Your internal pressure does. When it changes, timing returns. Flow returns.

Tool 5 — Beginner's Mind (Shoshin)

"I already know" closes the door to new information. "Let me find out what happens" leaves it open. Hold your current knowledge lightly enough to let it be refined. The surfer who approaches every session with something of a beginner's mind learns faster than the one defending their identity.

Tool 6 — Post-Session Check (Not a Verdict)

After the session, one question: Did I work on what I came to work on? Not: did I surf well? Not: were the waves good? If the objective disappeared after the first set — the session was enjoyable, but it was not training. Both are valid choices. The only mistake is confusing one for the other.

Coach Notes — Mental Tools Delivery

When to use these tools:

  • Student shows frustration → Tool 3 (frustration is information) + regression protocol
  • Student chases wave count, ignores mission → Tool 2 (one wave, one intention)
  • Student compares themselves to others → Tool 4 (expect nothing)
  • Student tight/anxious before session → Tool 1 (breath) + 30-second ritual
  • Student quits drill early on good waves → ego interference, name it directly

Do not deliver these as lectures. Use them as just-in-time interventions when the situation appears. Name the mechanism, give the tool, return to the mission.

Diagnostic & Corrections

One correction per wave. If the surfer needs more, the session focus is too complex. Use the Three Circles to diagnose.

Understanding Your Corrections

When your coach gives you a correction, it targets one specific thing. You do not need to fix everything at once. Focus on the one cue your coach gives you for the next wave. Trust the process — each correction builds on the last.

After each wave, ask yourself: Did I try what the coach asked? What did I feel? What changed? This self-assessment habit is one of the most important skills you are building at Yellow Belt.

Diagnostic Decision Trees

  • Cannot catch wave → Check position vs. pocket → timing → speed → sweet spot
  • Catches but poor ride → Check angle → cobra/line choice → posture → pocket proximity
  • Rides but cannot turn → Check eyes leading → front foot centered → kinetic chain → posture

Coaching Cue Library

Short, memorable, usable in water. 5 words or fewer. Triggers action, not analysis.

What the Cues Mean

During your water sessions, your coach will use short phrases — cues — to help you adjust in real time. These are not criticisms. They are precise signals designed to trigger one specific action without requiring you to think or analyze. When you hear a cue, respond with your body, not your mind.

The most common cues you will hear and what they mean:

  • "Angle the nose" — Point your board in the direction the wave is breaking before paddling
  • "Cobra first" — Do not skip the cobra position. Chest up, scan the wave, choose your line
  • "Eyes forward, hips down" — During pop-up, look where you are going and lower your center of gravity
  • "Back to the pocket" — You have drifted away from the energy source. Return toward the steepest part
  • "Starfish" — Use the controlled starfish dismount. Every time. Non-negotiable.

Pre-Session

One mission today. What is the mission? Where is the safe zone?

Paddle-Out

Form the T. Wait for the lull. Paddle wide. Where is the pocket?

Wave Approach

Angle the nose. Speed three. Now four. Three strokes earlier. Commit.

Pop-Up

Cobra first. Eyes forward, hips down. Front foot to the center. Get low.

Ride & Exit

Look where you want to go. Eyes first. Back to the pocket. Starfish. Reset. Sweet spot. Mission.

Post-Wave Feedback Protocol

Surfer speaks first. Coach asks three questions:

  • 1. "Did you attempt the mission?" — Binary check
  • 2. "What did you notice about the wave?" — Self-assessment
  • 3. "What needs adjustment?" — Surfer identifies correction

Coach adds ONE observation. Not two. One.

Cues to Avoid

  • "Do this, then this, then this" — Too many. One cue per wave.
  • "Relax!" — Replace with: "Breathe out. Drop your shoulders."
  • "That was wrong" — Replace with the specific correction.
  • "Be more confident" — Confidence is an outcome, not an input.
  • Any reference to the next belt. Stay in this session, this wave, this mission.

Progression & Adaptation

Progression is measured in observable behaviors, not days. Here's what advancement looks like and how to adapt the content to any delivery format.

Your Progression Path

Progression at Yellow Belt is not measured in days or sessions. It is measured in observable behaviors that appear across multiple sessions. Do not rush to the next stage. Trust that each step builds the foundation for what comes next.

What comes after Yellow Belt: At Blue Belt (Foundation), angled takeoffs are expected. Pocket awareness is habitual. Turns become linked. Named maneuver expressions begin. You are building a surfing vocabulary, not just surviving the wave.

Progression Stages

Early Yellow Belt (Seq. #6 Entry)

Paddles out through WW independently (even imperfect). Catches at least 1 green wave/session. Attempts angled takeoff. Identifies lefts and rights before paddling.

Mid Yellow Belt (Seq. #6 → #7 Transition)

Catches green waves consistently (more catches than misses). Visible paddling speed changes. Takes off closer to pocket more often. Turns intentionally. Venue analysis with minimal correction.

Late Yellow Belt (Approaching Blue Belt)

Rides along face with clear directional intent. Attempts vertical movement. Manages full session flow independently. Self-assesses without prompting. Recognizes when conditions exceed level.

Progression / Regression Rules

3 consecutive failures → Regress: reduce wave size → isolate step → move to land → return to whitewater

3 consecutive successes → Progress: add next step → increase wave selectivity → add constraint → shift to self-assessment → introduce next mission

Minimum Standard Gates

  • Gate 1 (Before green waves): Stable WW pop-up, automatic starfish, turtle roll attempted
  • Gate 2 (Before paddle angle): Can identify left vs. right from lineup
  • Gate 3 (Before turns): Catches green waves consistently with intentional paddle
  • Gate 4 (Before Seq. #7): Basic directional turns on most rides, manages session flow
