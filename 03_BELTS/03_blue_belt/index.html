Welcome to Blue Belt

You have completed your Yellow Belt. You catch green waves, ride the face with intention, manage your paddling speeds, and understand the pocket. Now everything deepens.

Blue Belt is where attempted surfing becomes reliable surfing. The transformation is not about learning entirely new things — it is about making existing skills consistent, repeatable, and connected. You will link sections of a wave together, generate speed through pumping and bottom turns, hold positions with intention, and execute the first named maneuvers of the TSS system.

The Core Shift at Blue Belt

At Yellow Belt, the surfer found the wave. At Blue Belt, the surfer owns the wave.

The environment doesn't change — it's still green waves. What changes is the surfer's relationship with the wave. They stop reacting and start controlling. They don't just ride — they link, hold, project, and recover. Blue Belt also marks the transition to full independence. The surfer owns their sessions. The coach shifts from direction to confirmation.

This belt builds three things:

  • Consistency — Reliable execution on both frontside and backside
  • Linking — Connecting multiple actions within a single wave
  • Full Independence — Session ownership, self-correction, tactical decision-making
Foundation — Before We Begin

ONE WAVE — Composure & Self-Correction

At Blue Belt, the mental demands are composure under complexity and self-correction as habit. Built on the ONE WAVE framework by Marcelo Castellanos.

Mental Tool — Composure Under Complexity

Multi-section rides, crowded lineups, and changing conditions demand calm. You do not rush decisions. You hold the bottom turn. You trust the projection. You maintain quality when the wave gets challenging.

Mental Tool — Self-Correction as Habit

After every wave, identify one thing to adjust on the next attempt. This happens internally, without coach prompting. It is not optional at Blue Belt — it is the skill that enables independent progression.

Mental Tool — Process Over Outcome

Evaluate rides by what you did, not by how it looked. A clean bottom turn with good projection that ends in a fall is more valuable than an accidental top turn that happens to land. The process is always the priority.

Mental Tool — Frustration Management in Plateau

Blue Belt is where plateaus first appear. You may feel stuck at Sequence #8 for weeks. Recognize that consistency takes time, not talent. The mental skill is not avoiding frustration — it is recognizing it as information and continuing to train within the method.

Coach Notes — Blue Belt Mental Framework

The primary mental shift: from coach-directed feedback to surfer-led debrief. The surfer identifies what went wrong first. The coach confirms or adjusts — adds one observation the surfer did not identify. If the surfer cannot self-correct after a wave, the problem is likely tactical (wrong choice) not technical (wrong execution).

When a Blue Belt surfer shows frustration during a plateau: do not add new techniques. Regress to the last reliable sequence and rebuild consistency there. Complexity before consistency is the primary cause of Blue Belt stagnation.

Block 0 — Surf Literacy

Wave Reading — Real-Time

At Blue Belt, wave reading shifts from pre-session to real-time. You read the wave during the ride: anticipating the pocket, predicting the wall, managing sections as they unfold.

From Pre-Read to Live-Read

At Yellow Belt you learned to read the wave before paddling. At Blue Belt, the read continues during the ride. You anticipate where the pocket will be in 2–3 seconds and position accordingly. You understand that the wave face changes shape as it breaks along its path.

  • Sections — The wave offers multiple sections as it breaks along its path. Each section is an opportunity.
  • Speed zones — The pocket = maximum energy. The shoulder = less power. The flat = speed dies.
  • Wall angle — Steeper wall = more speed. Flatter wall = need to pump or cutback.
Key Concept — The Flat Section

Create awareness of the wave's flat section and how reaching it results in a loss of speed. Sometimes, that is exactly what you want (setting up a cutback). Most of the time, it signals you've drifted too far from the pocket.

Wave Stages — Blue Belt Depth

Stage 1: Swell
Stage 2: Face forms
Stage 3: Pocket active
Stage 4: Whitewater

At Blue Belt you ride through Stage 2–3 continuously. When the pocket moves, you move with it. When sections close out ahead, you cutback. The wave is a resource you manage, not just ride.

Block 0 — Surf Literacy

Safety at Blue Belt

Safety is not a pillar — it is a non-negotiable operational layer across all levels. At Blue Belt, bigger waves demand deeper awareness.

Blue Belt Safety Parameters

  • Wave height: 3–6 feet (green waves). Overhead only with coach approval.
  • Equipment: Hard-top board appropriate for size and conditions. Involved in board selection.
  • Impact zone: Understand where not to paddle, not to sit, and how to recover after being caught inside.
  • Board control in wipeouts: Maintain board awareness during falls — cover head, do not release board carelessly.
  • Self-regulation: Fully autonomous. If conditions exceed comfort or ability, shorten the session or exit. No coach needs to tell you.

Communication Signals (Review)

  • ARE YOU OK? — Both arms triangle above head = OK
  • I NEED HELP: One arm waving side to side
  • OUT OF THE WATER: Coach crosses arms in X — return immediately

Supervision Model

Coach oversight, not coach direction. Ratio: 1 coach per 6–10 surfers depending on conditions. Coach provides strategic guidance, not wave-by-wave instruction.

Coach Notes — Safety at Blue Belt

The biggest risk at Blue Belt: the surfer pushes into conditions beyond ability because confidence outpaces judgment. Watch for surfers who paddle for waves they cannot manage. Autonomous self-regulation must be observed consistently, not just claimed.

Blocks 1–3 — Autonomous Foundations

Venue · Goal · Session Ownership

Blocks 1–3 (Preparation, Entry, Wave Interaction) are now autonomous. These were built at White and Yellow Belt. At Blue Belt, the surfer owns them completely.

What's Autonomous at Blue Belt

  • Venue Analysis — Independent. You read the spot, identify the peak, choose your positioning.
  • Goal Setting — You set your own session objectives. Specific, realistic, and strategic.
  • Warm-Up — Self-directed including breathwork (Bhastrika) and physical preparation.
  • Board & Paddle — All paddling speeds, duck dive, turtle roll are automatic.
  • Wave Interaction — Angled takeoff, pocket tracking, cobra + pick your line are all reliable.
Key Concept — Session Ownership

The surfer plans the session, executes it, debriefs it, and identifies priorities for the next session. The coach confirms or adjusts, but the surfer leads. This is not optional at Blue Belt — it is a graduation requirement.

Tactical Evolution at Blue Belt

  • Wave Selection — Based on what you can do with the wave, not just whether you can catch it.
  • Lineup Positioning — Read the lineup, identify the peak, adjust based on crowd and set patterns.
  • Mid-Ride Decisions — Pump for speed or set up bottom turn? Project to lip or cutback to pocket? These are live decisions.
  • Crowd & Priority — Fully autonomous. Manage priority, spacing, and communication in crowded lineups.

Coach Notes — Blocks 1–3

If Blocks 1–3 are not genuinely autonomous, the surfer was promoted to Blue Belt prematurely. Do not add Blue Belt complexity to a surfer still depending on you for venue reads or wave selection. Regress if needed — consistency before complexity.

What's your plan for this session? What did you adjust from last time? Why did you choose that wave?
Block 4 — Power Posture

Posture · Pumping · Recovery

At Blue Belt, posture becomes dynamic. It is not just the base — it is the engine of speed generation and the reset point after every action.

Dynamic Power Posture

The posture you learned at Yellow Belt is now the platform for dynamic movement. Every action begins from posture. Every action returns to posture. The quality of your posture determines the quality of everything that follows.

  • Chest toward the board's nose
  • Low center of gravity — 90° knee flex
  • Weight on front foot, back knee forward
  • Active scapula and elbow close to ribs
  • Shoulders on top of or forward of front foot

Pumping for Speed Generation

Pumping is coordinated compression and extension on the wave face. This is the active version of going up and down — generating speed through internal force instead of passively absorbing wave energy.

Frontside Pump

  • Start with posture
  • Lead with front hand, project oblique forward
  • Extend legs, keep weight forward
  • Swimming arm motion — elbow higher than head
  • Get low → back to posture

Backside Pump

  • Start with posture
  • Extend legs
  • Both arms toward direction, aligned with board nose
  • Switch rails with "M" motion — elbow and head point direction
  • Get back into posture

Diagnostic: When a Blue Belt surfer's ride dies after a maneuver, the first question is: did they recover? Did posture re-establish? Did they impulse for speed? Most "failed maneuvers" are actually failed recoveries.

Where's your posture after the turn? Impulse — get speed back! Front foot weight — reconnect with the board
Block 5 — Rotation + Bottom Turn

Bottom Turn · Hold · Rail Engagement

The bottom turn is the most important turn in surfing. It sets up everything. At Blue Belt, the medium bottom turn with hold is the primary training target.

Medium Bottom Turn

A controlled turn at the bottom of the wave face that engages the rail, generates speed, and creates a line of projection. "Medium" means clear rail engagement and directional intent — not a flat, open turn and not a full power carve (that's Purple Belt territory).

Frontside Bottom Turn

  • Weight on front foot
  • Elbow and forearm close to the water
  • Palm facing down
  • Engage obliques
  • HOLD

Backside Bottom Turn

  • Weight on front foot
  • Front arm at shoulder height
  • Fingertips close to the water
  • HOLD
Key Concept — HOLD

Hold means sustaining a position, line, or rail engagement for a controlled duration before releasing into the next action. Hold is what separates rushed surfing from intentional surfing. The bottom turn ends when projection begins — but only after the hold.

Rail Engagement

The board has two rails. Weighting one rail tilts the board and creates an arc. Rail engagement is what transforms flat riding into turning. At Blue Belt, you feel the rail engage and maintain that engagement through the turn.

The bottom turn is built from the P·R·C·H sequence: Posture → Rotation → Compression (hold) → and the Hold sustains through the apex before releasing into projection.

BB-06 to BB-09
Bottom Turn Types, Hold, Rail Engagement — Frontside & Backside
8-10 min

Types of Bottom Turns

The depth and arc of the bottom turn vary based on what you want to do next. A shallow bottom turn stays on the face for speed. A deeper bottom turn drops to the flat and projects back up with more power. Create awareness of the flat section — reaching it means speed loss, which is sometimes exactly what you need to set up a vertical projection.

Coach Notes — Block 5

Diagnostic: When a bottom turn has no projection, ask: did the rotation complete? Is the head leading the turn? Is the compression timed to the bottom of the wave? If the surfer rushes past the hold, projection will be weak and undirected.

Hold it longer! Where are you looking? Feel the rail — is it engaged? The bottom turn ends when projection begins
Block 6 — Projection

Projection · Direction · Energy Transfer

Projection is the direction and force that a bottom turn generates toward the next section of the wave. Without projection, a bottom turn goes nowhere.

What is Projection?

Projection is the energy release after the hold. The surfer projects toward a specific target on the wave face — the lip (for vertical maneuvers), the pocket (for cutbacks), or the shoulder (for speed lines). A good bottom turn without projection is a beautiful dead end.

Frontside Projection

  • Hips up and forward
  • Touch water + throw ball back
  • Keep scapula locked and chest out, pointing where you want to go
  • Weight on front foot

Backside Projection

  • Look at the target
  • Straighten front leg
  • Choke and point chest where you want to go
Key Concept — Energy First, Maneuvers Second

Inherited from Yellow Belt, deepened here. Maneuvers at Blue Belt are the consequence of good energy management, not the goal. A surfer with speed, a good bottom turn, and strong projection will execute maneuvers naturally. A surfer without energy will force maneuvers and fail.

Projection Targets

Where you project determines what maneuver follows. Projection is not random — it is a decision made during the hold.

Block 7 — Maneuvers

Named Sequence Expressions

Blue Belt introduces the first named actions in the TSS system. These are not isolated tricks — they are official named sequence expressions. Each one is a consequence of the blocks before it.

Key Concept — Rail Transitions

A maneuver is a change of direction, and direction is changed using the rails. To surf well, you must transition from rail to rail. Every named expression here is a rail transition built from the P·R·C·H sequence.

Frontside Expressions

  • Cruz Snap — After projection: "Cruz de Cristo" (chest aligned with or ahead of hips), look over shoulder, engage obliques, "Tirar Granada" (explosive energy release), return to posture.
  • Grenade — Maximum commitment version of the frontside snap. Projects through the turn with full power. At Blue Belt, introduced as a concept; consistency comes at Purple.
  • Cruz Cutback — A frontside cutback. When drifting to the shoulder, turn back toward the pocket. The primary tool for maintaining the pocket relationship through a full ride.

Frontside sequence: Bottom Turn → Hold → Projection → Cruz → Granada → Return to Posture

Backside Expressions

  • Choke — Backside compression. The surfer compresses through the turn with control and rail engagement, body facing away from the wave.
  • Tapaloco Snap — Backside snap executed from projection. The equivalent of Cruz Snap on the backside. "Tapaloco" + "Codazo" (elbow strike) drives the rotation.
  • Elbow — Upper-body engagement in the backside turn. The elbow and shoulder drive rotation when visual leading is limited.
  • Tapaloco Cutback — Backside cutback for linking sections. Turn back toward the pocket on backside waves.

Backside sequence: Bottom Turn → Hold → Choke → Tapaloco → Codazo → Impulse → Posture

Fundamental Maneuvers in Surfing

These maneuvers define the distance and the lines drawn on the wave's face, as well as the distance from the pocket:

  • Cutback — Return to the pocket when drifting to the shoulder
  • Carve — Fluid, arcing turn on the wave face
  • Re-entry — Hit the lip and return to the wave face
  • Foam Climb — Using the whitewater as a ramp

Frameworks

Three Circles of Power at Blue Belt

At Blue Belt, the Three Circles are no longer separate lenses. They operate as an integrated system. The surfer feels how body, board, and wave interact in real time.

Circle 1: Body — Rotation as Line Control

Rotation becomes the primary tool for line control. Compression and extension are now timed to the wave face shape for pumping and bottom turns. Hold — maintaining a position before releasing — becomes the skill that separates rushed surfing from intentional surfing.

Coach diagnostic: When a bottom turn has no projection — did the rotation complete? Is the head leading? Is the compression timed to the bottom of the wave?

Circle 2: Board — Rail Engagement

Rail engagement becomes the defining board skill. You experience how weighting one rail creates an arc, how foot placement pressure determines the turn radius, and how frontside/backside change the relationship between your body and the board's rails.

Coach diagnostic: When turns are flat and powerless — is the rail engaged? Is the back foot pressing the tail? Is the surfer riding on the flat bottom instead of the rail edge?

Circle 3: Wave — Real-Time Reading

You read the wave in real time. Anticipate where the pocket will be in 2–3 seconds and position accordingly. Understand that the wave face changes shape as it breaks along its path.

Coach diagnostic: When a surfer runs out of speed mid-ride — where are they relative to the pocket? Did they drift? Did they project to the flat?

The Infinite Circle at Blue Belt

At Blue Belt, every step of the Infinite Circle is functional and autonomous. The attention shifts from individual steps to the connections between them.

Multiple Loops Per Wave

This loop can repeat multiple times within a single wave. Each time you return to stable posture, you have the opportunity to rotate again, project again, and execute another action. That is the Infinite Circle — a repeating architecture with variations.

The mission of Blue Belt is to complete this circle reliably on both frontside and backside, and then link multiple circles within a single wave.

Coach Notes — Infinite Circle as Diagnostic Tool

When a surfer's execution breaks down during a ride, ask:

  • Is the problem in Block 4? — Is posture collapsing? Is the base unstable?
  • Is the problem in Block 5? — Is rotation incomplete? Is the rail not engaging?
  • Is the problem in Block 6? — Is there no projection? Is the surfer drifting without direction?
  • Is the problem in Block 7? — Is the maneuver failing because preceding blocks were incomplete?
  • Is the surfer failing to recover? — Speed lost because recovery doesn't happen?

Rule: The correct intervention targets the specific block that broke. The block before the failure point is usually where the real problem lives.

Practice

Missions (Water)

Progressive water missions that build the Blue Belt sequence from consistency through linking. Each mission adds one layer to the previous.

Mission 1 — Play with the Pocket

Pocket Awareness & Board Connection

  • Go into the water and play with the pocket: stay close, move away, return
  • Avoid getting caught in the whitewater
  • Focus on connecting feet to the board — good foot positioning throughout
Mission 2 — Connect with Board + Foot Positions

Foot Position Switches

  • Connect with the board, move foot to position 1 and return
  • Add impulse whenever you need speed (momentum)
  • Focus on deliberate foot position switches between position 1 and 2
Mission 3 — Pumping + Return to Pocket

Speed Generation

  • Perform a pump and return to the pocket
  • If the wave is fast, continue pumping to maintain speed
  • Practice frontside and backside pumping
  • Focus on compression/extension timing with the wave face
Mission 4 — Bottom Turn + Hold (Frontside)

Frontside Rail Engagement

  • Execute a frontside bottom turn with rail engagement
  • HOLD the compression at the bottom before releasing
  • Focus on vision: look where you want to project
  • Return to posture after the bottom turn
Mission 5 — Bottom Turn + Hold (Backside)

Backside Rail Engagement

  • Execute a backside bottom turn with rail engagement
  • HOLD — trust the rotation with your back to the wave
  • Focus on head turn leading the rotation
  • Return to posture after the bottom turn
Mission 6 — Bottom Turn + Projection (Frontside)

Energy Transfer

  • Bottom turn with hold, then project toward the lip or pocket
  • Practice: hips up, scapula locked, chest pointing at target
  • Feel the energy transfer from bottom turn to projection direction
  • Recover after projection: impulse + posture
Mission 7 — Bottom Turn + Projection (Backside)

Backside Projection

  • Backside bottom turn with choke
  • Project: look at target, straighten front leg, point chest
  • Recovery after projection
Mission 8 — Cruz Snap (Frontside)

First Named Expression

  • Bottom turn → hold → projection → Cruz Snap
  • Focus on the full sequence, not just the snap
  • Recovery: impulse + return to posture
Mission 9 — Tapaloco Snap (Backside)

Backside Named Expression

  • Bottom turn → hold → choke → Tapaloco Snap
  • Elbow engagement to drive the rotation
  • Recovery: return to posture
Mission 10 — Rail Transitions: Frontside Flow

Linking Frontside

  • Projection → Cruz → Grenade → return to posture
  • Focus on rail-to-rail transition fluidity
  • Each movement flows into the next — no stops between actions
Mission 11 — Rail Transitions: Backside Flow

Linking Backside

  • Choke → Tapaloco → Elbow → return to posture
  • Trust the rotation even without full visual leading
Mission 12 — Cruz Cutback (Frontside)

Return to Pocket

  • When drifting to the shoulder, execute Cruz Cutback
  • Turn back toward the pocket — re-engage the energy source
  • Continue riding after the cutback — linking sections
Mission 13 — Tapaloco Cutback (Backside)

Backside Return to Pocket

  • Backside cutback linking sections
  • Hold → Elbow → impulse → posture → continue riding
Mission 14 — Complete a Circle (Wave Completion)

The Full Infinite Circle

  • Complete one full Infinite Circle loop on a single wave
  • Posture → bottom turn → hold → projection → maneuver → recovery → posture
  • If the wave allows, attempt a second loop
  • The mission is the full architecture, not any single move
Practice

On-Land Drills

Connected-sequence practice on land. Drills at Blue Belt shift from single-skill practice to linked execution chains.

Drill L1

Stance + Rotation on Flat Ground

  • Find comfortable stance. Shoulders and hips forward, weight on front foot.
  • Practice rotation: look where you want to go, engage obliques, weight on front foot, foot centered on board.
  • Repeat 5–8 times per side.
Drill L2

Pop-Up + Foot Position Switches

  • Simulate 2-second pop-up + connect with board by lowering hips and looking forward. Repeat 5 times.
  • Pop-up → place foot in position 2 → switch to position 1 → add rotation → switch back to position 2 to generate speed. Repeat 5 times.
Drill L3

Rotation on Skateboard

  • Set up cones and chalk lines to define the practice area.
  • Start with frontside rotation, 4–8 times per person.
  • Repeat backside, 4–8 times per person.
  • Add turn + impulse + back to posture + continue straight.
  • Coach tip: Emphasize chest aligned over front foot or slightly forward.
Drill L4

Pumping on Skateboard

  • Frontside: Posture → projection + extend front leg → swimming arm motion → compress → back to posture. 10–20 reps without board, then on skateboard.
  • Backside: Posture → extend arms toward direction → extend legs → M motion rail switch → elbow/head pointing direction → back to posture. 10–20 reps, then on skateboard.
Drill L5

Bottom Turn + Projection (Flat Ground)

  • Start in posture. Simple rotation, flex slightly more than usual, HOLD. Add projection by extending legs and using the arm. Repeat 5 times.
  • Draw a "U" shape on the ground marking a projection point. Simulate movement following the line. Repeat 4 times per student.
Drill L6

Bottom Turn + Projection on Skateboard

  • Follow chalk line executing: Posture + Rotation + Hold + Projection.
  • Finish the maneuver upon completing the rotation.
  • Repeat 4–8 times depending on skill level.
Drill L7

Rail Transition Drill (Full Sequence)

  • Without skateboard: Bottom Turn + Hold → Projection → Cruz + Granada (FS) or Tapaloco + Codazo (BS) → Return to posture. Repeat multiple times.
  • With chalk line: Follow the path, execute each movement at correct point. 4 times per student.
  • On skateboard: Full sequence following chalk line. 4–8 times.
Drill L8

Guided Visualization

  • Visualize and focus on the chosen goal.
  • Guided group breathing session.
  • 5 minutes visualization practice.
Pool Drills

Paddling & Duck Dive

  • Practice efficient paddling mechanics in the pool.
  • Work on duck dive technique to improve wave entry.
  • Refine execution through practical drills.

Coach Notes — Drill Progression

Drills follow the same block logic as water practice. Start with isolated movements (L1–L2), add rotation (L3), add speed generation (L4), combine bottom turn + projection (L5–L6), then full sequence (L7). Never skip to full sequence drills before the components are reliable.

Mind & Emotions

Composure & Self-Correction

The primary psychological demands at Blue Belt are composure under complexity and self-correction as a daily habit.

Self-Improvement Tool: 3 Questions

Use these three questions after every session to discover what to improve:

1. What do I want to do but can't?

Example: I want to turn better but I lose speed.

2. Is it an execution or decision-making problem?

Execution: I'm not doing it correctly. Decision: I'm not choosing the right moment or place.

3. What is causing it?

Physical (strength, balance, endurance) · Mental (hesitation, fear, slow reactions) · Technical (mechanical details, timing) · Tactical (wave usage, reading, positioning)

Session Ownership Protocol

  • Before session: Set one specific focus. Not five objectives — one focus.
  • During session: Self-correct between waves. Adjust approach without coach prompting.
  • After session: Lead the debrief. Articulate what worked, what needs adjustment, and next priority.
  • Before leaving: State next-session priority.

Coach Notes — Mental Framework

Feedback protocol: Surfer-led debrief with coach confirmation. Coach adds one observation the surfer did not identify. Surfer states next-session priority before leaving. Not more — not less.

Video Analysis: Focus on line drawn on wave face, rail engagement angle, bottom turn depth, projection direction, recovery quality. The surfer reviews own footage first and identifies three things to adjust. Coach adds one observation. Not more.

Coach Tools

Diagnostic & Corrections

Block-based diagnostic framework. Identify the block where execution breaks down and correct the block before the failure point.

Diagnostic Rule

The block before the failure point is usually where the real problem lives. If a maneuver fails (Block 7), check projection (Block 6). If projection is weak, check bottom turn and hold (Block 5). Work backward until you find the root cause.

Block 4 Failures — Power Posture / Recovery

Problem: Ride dies after maneuver
Check: Did they recover? Did posture re-establish? Did they impulse?
Problem: Surfer can't generate speed
Check: Is pumping timed to wave face? Are hips and ankles engaged? Compression/extension rhythm?

Block 5 Failures — Bottom Turn / Hold

Problem: Bottom turn has no projection
Check: Did rotation complete? Is head leading? Is compression timed to bottom of wave?
Problem: Turns are flat and powerless
Check: Is the rail engaged? Is the back foot pressing tail? Riding on flat bottom vs rail edge?
Problem: Rushed bottom turn
Check: Did they HOLD? Hold is sustaining through the apex before releasing. Most Blue Belt surfers release too early.

Block 6 Failures — Projection

Problem: No direction after bottom turn
Check: Did they choose a target? Is the problem in Block 5 (incomplete hold)?
Problem: Weak projection
Check: Are hips releasing up and forward? Is the scapula locked? Chest pointing at target?

Block 7 Failures — Maneuvers

Problem: Maneuver disconnected from bottom turn
Check: Was there projection between bottom turn and maneuver? If not, the maneuver is isolated.
Problem: Can't link sections
Check: Is recovery happening? Does the surfer return to posture after each action? Linking = recovery.

Common Mistakes at Blue Belt

  • Rushing complexity before consistency: Attempting maneuvers before the bottom turn is reliable.
  • Avoiding backside: Spending disproportionate time on frontside. Check frontside/backside distribution.
  • Forcing maneuvers without energy: Attempting snaps without speed, bottom turn, or projection.
  • No recovery habit: One action per wave instead of linking because recovery never happens.
  • Confusing style with technique: Adding personal flair before the mechanical sequence is reliable.

Progression & Promotion

Progression markers, promotion criteria, and tracking scales for Blue Belt. Binary checklist — every item must be met for promotion to Purple Belt.

Progression Markers

Early Blue Belt (Sequences #8–#9)

  • Completes recognizable frontside rides on most catchable waves
  • Attempts backside with intention, even if less consistent
  • Pumping is functional: generates visible speed
  • Takes off with correct angle automatically on both sides
  • Session independence is full

Mid Blue Belt (Sequences #10–#11)

  • Medium bottom turns with visible rail engagement on frontside
  • Backside bottom turns show intentional rail engagement
  • Projects after bottom turn toward recognizable target
  • First maneuver attempts show connection to bottom turn
  • Recovers after maneuvers: posture, speed, re-engage

Late Blue Belt (Sequences #12–#13)

  • Links two or more named actions on a single wave
  • Executes backside linking with functional quality
  • Cutback is active on both sides when drifting to shoulder
  • Wave completion is the norm, not the exception
  • Self-correction visible between waves
  • Can articulate line plan before paddling for a wave

Promotion Criteria → Purple Belt (Emerging)

Binary checklist. Every item must be met. Coach sign-off required. Autonomy criteria evaluated across multiple sessions.

Consistency & Riding

  • Frontside green waves with consistent connected sequence
  • Backside green waves with functional consistency
  • Speed through pumping on most rides
  • Medium bottom turns with rail engagement on both sides
  • Hold during bottom turns: compression maintained before projection

Projection & Maneuvers

  • Projects from bottom turn with clear directional intent
  • Executes Cruz Snap (FS) and Tapaloco Snap (BS) as consequence of full sequence
  • Recovers after maneuvers: posture + speed through impulse

Linking & Wave Completion

  • Links two or more named actions on a single wave regularly
  • Rides from takeoff to natural end (wave completion)
  • Executes Cruz Cutback (FS) and Tapaloco Cutback (BS)

Independence & Decision-Making

  • Wave selection based on tactical criteria
  • Full session management independently across multiple sessions
  • Self-corrects without coach prompting (observed consistently)
  • Manages priority and etiquette autonomously in moderate crowds
  • Makes safe decisions independently including self-regulation

Physical & Mental

  • Sustains 90–120 minute sessions with consistent performance
  • Manages frustration during plateaus without abandoning the method
  • Leads session debrief: articulates what worked, what needs adjustment, next priority

Tracking Scales

  • Focus (1–5): Session mission adherence
  • Composure (1–5): How well surfer maintains composure across session
  • Control (1–5): Overall ride quality and consistency
  • Autonomy (1–5): Self-directed with minimal coach intervention
  • Linking (1–5): 1 = single-turn rides. 3 = two sections. 5 = full wave completion with multiple linked actions

Red Flags / Non-Promotion Indicators

  • Surfer cannot complete a frontside ride without coach direction
  • Bottom turns show no rail engagement after sustained training
  • No self-correction between waves despite repeated coaching
  • Surfer avoids one side entirely (frontside or backside)
  • Surfer cannot manage a session independently

What Comes Next: Purple Belt (Emerging)

At Purple, the surfer moves from consistency to expression. Maneuver variety increases. The surfer develops a personal surfing identity. The fundamentals from Blue Belt are the technical reliability that makes expression possible.

Blue Belt prepares the foundation for the question: "Now that you can surf consistently, who are you as a surfer?"
