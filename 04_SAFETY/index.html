Safety Canon v2.0

Safety as Applied Intelligence

The governing safety document for The Surf Sequence® methodology. It defines the philosophical standard, the decision standard, the operational baseline, and the minimum professional obligations for delivering surf coaching under the method with control, clarity, and duty of care.

Version 2.0
Scope All Belts · All Venues
Authority Canon — Tier 2
Governing Doctrine

The Surf Sequence® does not treat safety as a legal appendix or a list of restrictions. Safety is structural. It is part of how the session is designed, how progression is sequenced, how the environment is read, how the group is led, and how the coach protects learning quality while reducing unnecessary exposure.

Architecture of the Canon

  • Part I — Foundation: philosophy, principles, and the method's definition of safety
  • Part II — Roles and duty of care: responsibilities of coaches, staff, students, and guardians
  • Part III — Risk logic: student profile, condition matching, venue analysis, and pre-session assessment
  • Part IV — Session safety system: briefing, land-water-exit standards, equipment, spacing, communication
  • Part V — Progression safety: exposure thresholds and adaptation by level
  • Part VI — Incident and emergency canon: conflict, medical events, ocean hazards, escalation rules
  • Part VII — Culture and environment: etiquette, conduct, hygiene, and professional prevention culture
  • Part VIII — Indexed operational guide: what to do in specific incidents, conflicts, and emergencies

Safety as Part of Coach Excellence

In The Surf Sequence®, a coach is not excellent merely because technique cues are accurate. Excellence includes the ability to select the right venue, reject the wrong venue, brief clearly, position the group correctly, regulate intensity, identify fatigue and fear, manage conflict, adapt drills, and know precisely when to stop, relocate, delay, or cancel.

Safety as Part of Learning

A quality surf session combines progression, challenge, clarity, and psychological regulation. Safety creates the focus that allows information to be absorbed and repeated. Ocean reading, board handling, safe falling, spacing, entry and exit habits, recall protocols, and emotional regulation are taught as learning content, not as secondary warnings.

Part I — Foundation
Core Safety Principles

Non-negotiable principles that govern all delivery formats within the method. They shape every lesson design and field decision.

Principle 1

Safety before performance

No wave or maneuver opportunity is worth sacrificing group control.

Principle 2

Read before acting

Coaches observe ocean, beach, people, equipment, and the social environment before entering.

Principle 3

Progression before exposure

Complexity is earned through control, not ambition.

Principle 4

Control before complexity

The board, body, breath, space, and group must be stable before challenge increases.

Principle 5

Adaptation over ego

If conditions are wrong, the session changes.

Principle 6

Shared responsibility

The coach leads; students must report issues honestly and follow safety instructions.

Principle 7

Calm authority

Safety decisions are communicated early, clearly, and without emotional leakage.

Principle 8

Prevention before reaction

The best intervention happens before the incident begins.

Part II — Roles
Responsibility Structure & Duty of Care

Safety authority and safety accountability must be explicit. Every operating environment must assign roles before the session begins.

Duty of Care Standard

The operating team must take reasonable and proactive measures to protect students from foreseeable harm while preserving the integrity of learning. This includes student screening, venue selection, condition matching, supervision, equipment readiness, group control, and timely escalation.

Head Coach / Lead

  • Final authority on go / no-go decisions
  • Pre-session venue analysis signoff
  • Group composition and ratio approval
  • Incident escalation and reporting review
  • Emergency contacts confirmed

Session Coach

  • Delivers beach briefing
  • Maintains visual control of group at all times
  • Makes real-time stop/adapt/relocate decisions
  • Administers first-response if needed
  • Completes post-session incident report

Assistant Coach

  • Supports group control, especially tail students
  • Manages equipment pre and post session
  • Maintains secondary communication device
  • Accompanies patient to medical if needed

Student / Guardian

  • Report injuries, fear, medication, conditions honestly
  • Follow coach commands immediately once session is active
  • Respect assigned zones and recall signals
  • Do not exceed approved exposure level

Part II — Risk Logic
Student Profile & Safe Admission

No student should enter a session solely on the basis of what they say they want to do. Entry must be based on what they can control.

Minimum Student Intake

  • Age and guardian status when applicable
  • Self-reported and observed confidence in water
  • Swimming ability and ocean awareness
  • Current injuries, recent surgery, asthma, panic history, medication, or medical restrictions
  • Emotional profile: responsiveness, fear threshold, and ability to follow instructions
  • Current goal and expected challenge level
Risk Classification Rule

Risk is never classified by label alone. The same student may be low risk in white water and high risk in shoulder-high crowded surf. A control-based matrix combining surfer capability and environmental demand must always be used.

Part III — Pre-Session
Venue Analysis Protocol

Venue analysis is mandatory before entering the water. The beach is not only a safety site; it is the method's teaching environment.

Mandatory Analysis Points

  • Type of break and likely impact zone
  • Entry and exit routes; channels where available
  • Current lines, sweep direction, and zones of trapped movement
  • Set rhythm, lulls, reform sections, and points of line-up compression
  • Bottom composition: sand, reef, rock, mixed surface, or hidden hazards
  • Crowd pattern, learner clusters, board type density, and local hierarchy
  • Physical hazards: hooks, nets, debris, exposed rock, vehicles, dogs, restricted zones
  • Medical access: rescue point, meeting point, nearest road, and emergency destination
Social Hazards — Coach Awareness

Identify local tension, fishermen, vehicle exposure, or restricted zones. Protect the group from ego-driven decisions by staff or students. Avoid unnecessary crowd compression and route selection that invites conflict.

Part III — Pre-Session
Pre-Session Risk Assessment

Before the first student enters the water, the coach must answer one governing question.

Governing Question

Do these conditions allow teaching with real control today?

If the answer is unclear, the answer is no.

Coach Operating Priorities

  • Protect the group before chasing performance opportunities
  • Read conditions before entering and continue reading them during the session
  • Match challenge to actual student control, not to stated ambition
  • Keep commands short, calm, and repeatable
  • Adapt early; do not wait for disorder
  • Record incidents and convert them into prevention learning

Part III — Pre-Session
Beach Briefing Protocol

The briefing converts analysis into shared order. Every session requires a beach briefing before entry.

Mandatory Briefing Elements

  • Objective of the session and success criteria
  • Where to enter, where to stay, and where to exit
  • Which waves are for this group and which are not
  • Recall signal: what it means and what to do when given
  • What to do if board is lost, if fear spikes, or if current pulls sideways
  • Rotation: how the group will rotate, wait, and reset
  • Prohibited behavior in this session
Communication Standard

The method requires standardized command language. In wind, whitewater, noise, and stress, long explanations fail. The coach uses short, pre-taught commands and visible arm signals.

Part IV — Session System
Land · Entry · Water · Exit

Safety standards for every phase of the session — from board assignment to final headcount.

Land Safety

Boards assigned by level, leashes checked, fins verified, sunscreen and hydration addressed, warm-up completed, transport handled with control. Group movement on the beach preserves order and visibility.

Water Entry

Entry occurs only through the designated route. If a channel exists, the group uses it. Entry is sequenced — never a chaotic rush. Students do not enter until commands, zones, and spacing are understood.

In-Water Control

The coach must keep the group visible, readable, and recoverable. Positioning is not random. Distance between students reflects board size, current, and skill. One student's confusion must never compromise the whole group.

Exit Safety

Exits are managed with the same seriousness as entries. The group exits through the agreed route, boards remain under control, and final count is completed before the session is considered closed.

In-Session Group Control — Coach
  • Keep the group visible and compact; do not coach one surfer so deeply that the rest become unmanaged
  • Reposition early if drift, crowd, or side current starts stretching the group
  • When conditions become unstable, reduce complexity first: smaller zone, fewer variables, simpler task
  • Do not mix incompatible exposure levels in the same teaching pocket
  • If one student becomes the dominant risk variable, reduce their exposure or remove them from the session

Part IV — Session System
Equipment & Communication

Equipment Safety Standard

Equipment must match skill level and conditions. Every session requires a minimum equipment check: boards assigned by size and experience, leashes functional, fins secure, and appropriate wetsuits/rash vests for identification and protection.

Command Language

The method requires standardized communication. In the field, the coach uses short commands that can be understood under noise and stress. Signal consistency matters more than verbal sophistication.

INSIDE! OUT! HOLD! RETURN! REGROUP! STOP!

Part V — Progression Safety
Adaptation, Fatigue & Overexposure

A session should be adjusted before the environment forces it to fail.

Warning Signs

The coach must watch for fatigue, respiratory stress, freezing, emotional flooding, repeated confusion, or declining board control.

Non-Negotiable Rules
  • Do not let output collapse before intervening
  • Do not escalate exposure to prove resilience
  • Do not confuse bravery with readiness
  • Do not continue if fear prevents learning and basic control
  • Do not shame fear — regulate it
  • Do not reward overconfidence with more exposure

Part VI — Emergency Canon
Indexed Emergency & Conflict Guide

Use in four moves: identify the situation, stabilize the scene, apply the case protocol, and document/escalate when control is not fully restored.

Response Sequence

1. Stabilize people before equipment.  2. Protect the group from secondary risk.  3. Communicate with short commands only.  4. Escalate early when control is doubtful.  5. Record the incident before end of shift.

Medical / Injury

Case — Head / Face Impact

All head injuries are treated as serious. Remove from water immediately. No return to water that day. Monitor for delayed concussion signs: confusion, nausea, unequal pupils. Escalate to emergency services if loss of consciousness, seizure, or disorientation is present.

Case — Severe Panic / Respiratory Distress

Get student to shore or stable position. Calm, clear commands. Remove from session. If breathing does not normalize, activate emergency services. Never leave panicked student unattended in water.

Case — Laceration / Bleeding

Remove from water. Apply direct pressure. If bleeding is severe or wound is deep, activate emergency transport. Keep student calm. Do not allow return to water.

Ocean / Conditions

Case — Sudden Current Change / Drift

Recall group immediately. Account for all students. Reposition or relocate. If a student is caught in current, coach directs them to paddle parallel to shore, not against current. Do not send multiple rescuers unless trained.

Case — Conditions Exceed Group Level

Extract weakest student first. Recall group. Reduce zone or relocate entirely. Do not "wait it out" hoping conditions improve.

Behavior / Conflict

Case — Intoxicated / Defiant Student

Students who are intoxicated, repeatedly defiant, or openly disruptive should not enter the water. Remove from group. Escalate to head coach or management privately, away from the group.

Case — Local Conflict / External Aggression

Do not confront angry locals in the water. De-escalate and relocate. Protect the group from ego-driven decisions. Leave venue if necessary. Safety of students always overrides venue preference.

Part VI — Emergency Canon
Stop · Adapt · Relocate · Cancel

The canon requires decisive thresholds, not vague intuition.

Stop

When there is a loss of group control, panic, injury concern, or a fast-rising hazard.

Adapt

When the session can remain safe by reducing complexity, shrinking the zone, or lowering exposure.

Relocate

When the teaching objective is still valid but the current zone no longer provides control.

Cancel

When safety depends on hope, luck, or repeated rescue behavior rather than coaching structure.

Escalation Thresholds — Escalate Without Delay When:
  • You no longer have continuous visual control of the full group
  • A head/neck injury, loss of consciousness, breathing issue, or severe panic is suspected
  • Conditions have shifted beyond the demonstrated level of the weakest student in the water
  • A conflict is affecting student safety or emotional stability
  • Staff roles are unclear during an active incident
  • Emergency equipment, communications, or transport capability are compromised

Part VI — Emergency Canon
Emergency Action Plan Integration

Every operating environment must connect this canon to a local Emergency Action Plan.

Required Local Plan Elements

  • Emergency roles assigned to specific personnel
  • Rescue route and meeting point defined
  • External contacts: lifeguard, hospital/clinic, police
  • Medical destination with estimated response time
  • Communication chain: who calls whom
  • Documentation responsibilities post-incident

First-Aid Response Framework

This canon does not replace medical certification. It requires that coaches maintain current first-aid competence. The framework is: stabilize → protect airway and spine → control bleeding → regulate heat → remove from further danger → refer without delay when severity exceeds coaching scope.

Incident Reporting Standard

Every incident that disrupts normal session flow, requires special intervention, creates risk of recurrence, or involves injury must be recorded. Reporting is not only defensive; it is preventive. The purpose is to improve future decision quality.

Part VII — Culture
Etiquette, Environment & Prevention Culture

Surf Etiquette as Safety

Etiquette is not cosmetic culture. It is collision prevention, social de-escalation, and line-up order. Priority, paddling wide, controlling the board, and respecting locals are safety behaviors.

Environmental Safety

Respect for the ocean and the beach is part of safe delivery. Waste, damaged gear left on the beach, careless vehicle loading, and disregard for local access norms all increase operational risk and erode professional credibility.

Professional Culture of Prevention

The Surf Sequence® expects calm leaders, accurate communication, good presentation, punctuality, sober operation, clean equipment handling, and disciplined follow-through. Prevention culture is visible in routine.

Field Operations
Coach Alignment Formations

Tactical positioning for group control. Select a formation to see roles, capacity, and deployment rules. Flags mark the zone boundaries in width and depth.

La Dupla

2 instructors — up to 6 students in safe conditions.

Instructor A — Limit Position

Stays at the safe zone boundary. Prevents students from passing into the impact zone. Assists with wave selection, turning, and choosing the right foam or wave.

Instructor B — Shore Position

Receives students at shore. Assists with return to Instructor A position. Provides technique feedback during reset.

Zone Control

Flags mark the boundaries in both width and depth. Students must remain within the flagged corridor at all times.

El Triángulo

3 instructors — larger groups with full coverage.

Instructors A & B — Limit Positions

Both positioned at the safe zone boundary to prevent students reaching the impact zone. They assist with wave selection and give in-water instructions.

Instructor C — Feedback Position

Positioned between the teaching zone and shore. Provides technique feedback. Assists students returning to A and B positions. Manages the reset cycle.

El Imán

1 instructor — up to 4 students if conditions and student level allow.

Mission #1 for All Students

Always return to Instructor A. This is the non-negotiable rule of this formation. The instructor must transmit and verify this rule clearly before entering the water.

Instructor A — Central Magnet

Positioned in the teaching zone as the gravitational center. All students orbit around and always return to this position. Coach must confirm every student is capable of returning independently before deployment.

Capacity Limit

Maximum 4 students only if conditions are safe and all students demonstrate the ability to self-return. Reduce immediately if any student struggles.

Red Code & Plan B Protocol

When the ocean represents a danger to any student or instructor, the water session must be cancelled immediately.

🚨 Red Code Definition

Swell exceeding 10 feet, or any hazard that compromises the safety of students or instructors = CANCEL THE CLASS.

Environmental Hazards — Automatic Red Code

The following conditions trigger Red Code regardless of swell size:

Jellyfish

Stingrays

Exposed rocks / reef

Sharks / Crocodiles

Venomous creatures

Contaminated water

Boat / vessel traffic

Orca / whale activity

Lightning / storm

Plan B — Alternative Session Options

When Red Code is activated, the class is not cancelled — the venue changes. Select the best Plan B based on available resources:

Surf Skate Session

Practice turns, compression, and flow on dry land. Ideal for all levels.

Pool Paddling

Paddling technique, pop-up drills, breath control in controlled water environment.

Gym / Functional Training

Surf-specific fitness: balance, core, flexibility, endurance. Use pillars FÍS framework.

Video Analysis

Review session footage. Identify block-level corrections. Connect theory to execution.

Theory / Block 0

Ocean reading, wave types, etiquette, safety signals. Reinforce surf literacy.

Games (Kids Programs)

Structured play that builds surf awareness. "Evolve through play" methodology applies.

Student Safety Guide
Student Rules & Quick Check

Clear rules for learning, behaving well, and progressing safely in The Surf Sequence®.

How TSS Views Safety

Safety is not there to slow you down. It is there to help you learn faster, with better decisions, better awareness, and more consistent progress.

Your Responsibilities as a Student

  • Tell the truth about injuries, fear, fatigue, medication, or anything that could affect your session
  • Follow the coach's commands quickly and without debate once the session is active
  • Respect the level you are currently in — do not jump exposure just to prove something
  • Care for your board and do not let it become a danger to others
  • Respect the ocean, the beach, and the people surfing around you

Before Entering the Water

  • Listen to the beach briefing and ask questions before entry
  • Know where to enter, where to stay, where not to go, and where to exit
  • Check your leash, board, and protective gear
  • Understand the recall signal and what to do when given

In the Water

  • Stay with the group and inside the assigned zone
  • Hold onto your board whenever possible and protect your head during falls
  • Do not paddle into waves your coach has not approved for your level
  • If you lose control, drift, or get scared — stay calm and follow one instruction at a time
What to Do If...
  • You lose your board: Protect your head, swim to shore, alert your coach
  • You feel scared: Signal the coach, stay calm, float and breathe
  • You drift out of zone: Paddle parallel to shore, not against current
  • You hear the recall signal: Return to shore immediately through the agreed route
  • Someone else is in trouble: Alert the coach — do not attempt rescue unless trained
