About The Surf Sequence

Preface

Why This Book Exists

Nobody teaches surfers how to train.

That is not a complaint. It is an observation — one I've made over thousands of hours on the beach, watching talented athletes dissolve what were supposed to be training sessions into free surfing within twenty minutes of paddling out. Not because they lacked dedication. Not because they didn't care about improving. But because nobody had ever explained the difference between being in the water and actually working on something while you're there.

There are countless resources on surf technique. On maneuvers, on paddling mechanics, on reading waves. What doesn't exist — or didn't, until now — is a guide to the mental architecture of learning itself. How to focus. How to design a session with intention. How to move through the inevitable frustration of improvement without letting it derail you. How to build the internal conditions that allow real progress to happen.

That is what this book is about.

I came to this understanding not just through surfing, but through a lifetime of competition and learning. Swimming. Greco-Roman wrestling. Paintball. Jiu-jitsu. Years working alongside psychologists, breathing specialists, strength coaches, and sports scientists. Thousands of hours coaching surfers from complete beginners to Olympic athletes. Every competition I entered, every loss I processed, every athlete I guided through a breakthrough or a breakdown — all of it lives inside this book.

In 2024, I watched Bryan Pérez become El Salvador's first Olympic surfer at the Paris Games. That result did not come from technique alone. It came from everything this book describes — the mental framework, the structured training, the ability to stay present and focused under the highest pressure that exists in our sport.

This book is the conceptual foundation of The Surf Sequence Method — a methodology I've spent a lifetime building. Not just the years at Puro Surf, not just the decade coaching Olympic athletes. A lifetime. This is not the technical manual. The drills, the progressions, the specific training sequences — those come later. This book is what comes first: the mental framework that makes everything else work. Read it as a surfer who wants to improve. Read it as a coach who wants to teach more effectively. Either way, what you build here — in how you think about learning — is the foundation everything else is built on.

In his book The ONE Thing, Gary Keller makes the case that the brain learns best when it has a single, clear focus. Not five objectives. Not a general intention to surf well. One thing. One wave. One specific intention carried into the water with full attention.

That idea sits at the center of everything that follows.

This book will not make you a better surfer overnight. What it will do is change how you approach the water — so that every session becomes an opportunity for deliberate improvement rather than another hour of doing what you already know how to do.

One wave. One intention.

PART I

THE INNER GAME

Before you can improve your surfing, you need to prepare the tool you use to learn: your mind.

Chapter 1

Be Here and Now: The Art of Focus

I was ten years old when a psychologist handed me a piece of paper with a circle drawn on it and a small dot at the center.

"Focus on the dot," she said. "Just the dot. Nothing else."

I stared at it. Within seconds, the circle began to fade. Not disappear — fade. The edges softened and receded until there was almost nothing left except the dot I was looking at. When I let my attention drift to the circle again, it came back immediately, sharp and clear. When I returned to the dot, it faded again.

"That," she said, "is how focus works. When your attention goes to one point, everything around it becomes background. When attention divides, noise returns. When attention centers, noise fades."

I had been diagnosed with ADHD. My mind, left to itself, moved constantly — from idea to idea, from one thing to the next, never settling. That session with the psychologist was the first time I understood that focus was not something you had or didn't have. It was something you trained. A skill. A practice. Something you could get better at — and something that, without practice, would work against you.

That circle and dot exercise has stayed with me for twenty-seven years. And it is the foundation of everything in this chapter.

---

The Two Directions the Mind Escapes

The mind is not designed for the present moment. It is designed for survival — which means it is constantly scanning for what might go wrong in the future and processing what went wrong in the past. This was useful when the primary concern was predators and food sources. In the water, trying to improve your surfing, it creates problems.

When you paddle out with your mind in the future — worrying about whether this session will be good, whether you'll execute the maneuver you've been working on, whether that person watching from the beach will see you fall — you are not actually surfing. You are managing anxiety while surfing.

When you paddle out with your mind in the past — replaying the wave you messed up twenty minutes ago, comparing today to a great session you had last week, judging yourself for a mistake you made in your last competition — you are not actually surfing either. You are carrying dead weight into the water.

The only moment in which learning can happen is now. This wave. This moment. This specific feedback from the board under your feet and the water moving beneath you.

Everything else is noise.

Take Out the Trash

Before a session, most surfers don't think about what they're bringing into the water with them. They arrive at the beach, maybe do a quick stretch, wax the board, and paddle out — carrying everything that happened that day, that week, that month. A difficult conversation. A frustrating commute. A comparison to someone better. An expectation about how the session should go.

All of that is in the water with you.

"Taking out the trash" means deliberately clearing that mental clutter before you paddle out. Not suppressing it — that doesn't work. But acknowledging it and choosing to set it down before entering the water.

The ocean doesn't care about your day. It doesn't adjust its waves for your mood. But you can choose what you bring to it.

Curiosity as the Optimal Learning State

There is a specific mental state that is more conducive to learning than any other. Not determination. Not intensity. Not even concentration in its traditional sense.

Curiosity.

When you are genuinely curious — when you are asking "what happens if I try this?" rather than "will I succeed at this?" — the nervous system opens. Dopamine is released. Pattern recognition improves. The brain becomes more receptive to new information.

The surfer who enters the water thinking "I need to land this maneuver today" is in a fundamentally different state than the surfer who enters thinking "I wonder what happens when I shift my weight earlier in this section." The first is performing. The second is learning.

Performance and learning are not the same thing. Sometimes they conflict. The surfer trying to look good — to themselves or to others — is usually not learning at the optimal rate. The surfer who is genuinely interested in what happens next is.

Breath as Navigation

The breath is the only part of the autonomic nervous system you can consciously control. That makes it unusually powerful as a tool for managing mental state.

When the mind drifts into the future or the past — into anxiety or regret — the breath typically becomes shallow and fast. When you consciously slow and deepen the breath, the nervous system responds. Heart rate lowers. Attention narrows. The sense of urgency recedes.

I learned this not just from reading about it but from ice baths. Getting into very cold water teaches you something immediate about the relationship between breath and state: if you fight the cold — if you tense up, breathe fast, try to push through — the discomfort becomes overwhelming. If you slow the breath deliberately, something shifts. The cold doesn't change. Your relationship to it does.

The ocean offers the same lesson on a different scale. You cannot control the waves. You can control the breath that determines how you meet them.

The 30-Second Pre-Session Ritual

Before you paddle out — every session, every time:

Sit or stand still for thirty seconds. Ask yourself three questions: Where am I? (Here, on this beach, in this water.) What time is it? (Now, this moment.) Then take three slow, deliberate breaths.

Then choose one thing — one specific focus for this session. Not a goal. A focus. Something you will pay attention to while you surf.

That is the entire ritual. Thirty seconds. Three breaths. One focus.

It seems small. Over hundreds of sessions, it becomes the difference between a surfer who improves and a surfer who simply accumulates time in the water.

Chapter 2

Free Surfing vs. Training: One Wave, One Intention

She was one of the most talented junior surfers I had worked with. Quick, intuitive, naturally powerful. She had competed at a high level and won. And she had hit a wall.

"I don't understand," she told me before a session. "I've been surfing more than ever. I feel like I'm working harder than everyone around me. But I'm not improving."

I watched her surf for an hour. What I saw was someone who was working very hard at doing what she already knew how to do — executing her existing repertoire with intensity and passion, but without any specific focus on what needed to change.

At the end of the session, I identified one thing: her front foot was landing slightly off-center on her pop-up. Not dramatically — maybe two or three inches. But that small misalignment was creating a chain reaction through her entire surfing. Her posture, her balance, her ability to generate speed through turns — all of it was being limited by this one foundational issue.

"Next session," I told her, "I want you to work on one thing only. Your front foot placement on the pop-up. Nothing else. When a good wave comes, your job is not to surf the best wave of your life. Your job is to land your front foot in the right position. That's the whole mission."

She looked at me like I had asked her to do something boring.

One session later, her posture had already improved. Three sessions later, her balance was noticeably more stable. Two weeks later, without ever directly working on her speed or her turns, both had improved significantly — because the foundational issue was fixed.

One thing. That was it.

---

Two Different Activities

Free surfing and training are not the same activity. This sounds obvious. It is not treated that way.

Free surfing is expression. It is using the tools you already have — your current skill set, your existing repertoire — to enjoy the ocean. The intention is loose. The evaluation is felt rather than measured. There is no specific objective beyond riding waves and experiencing whatever that brings. Free surfing is valuable. It is joyful. It is part of why surfers surf.

Training is intervention. It is deliberately introducing something new — a change in technique, a new movement pattern, a different approach to reading the wave — with the specific intention of building a capability that doesn't fully exist yet. Training has a specific objective. It has a defined limit — a time period or a number of repetitions. And it has a simple evaluation afterward: did I stay on the objective?

The problem is not that surfers free surf. The problem is that most surfers call everything they do in the water "training" — and then wonder why their improvement is slow.

The Switch Cost

When the mind shifts between tasks, it pays an energy cost. Neuroscientists call this the "switch cost" — the cognitive tax of moving attention from one objective to another. In a controlled environment, this cost is measurable. In a high-variable environment like the ocean, where conditions are constantly changing and decisions happen in fractions of seconds, this cost is significant.

If you enter the water with ten objectives — work on your pop-up, improve your bottom turn, try that new snap in the shoulder, work on reading the sets, practice your paddle positioning — your mind is paying ten switch costs every time it moves between them. That is ten opportunities for the focus to break down completely and for the session to drift into automatic surfing: doing what you already know how to do, reinforcing what's already there, improving nothing.

One objective. The switch cost drops to nearly zero. Full attention can go to one specific thing.

The Ego's Interference

Here is what I see with every surfer I have ever coached, including Olympic athletes:

They start the session with an objective. A good wave appears. And in the moment of that wave, they forget the objective entirely and try to surf their best possible wave.

This is not a failure of discipline. It is the ego doing exactly what it is designed to do — protecting the self-image. A good wave is an opportunity to look good, to feel competent, to confirm the story of who you are as a surfer. Working on a specific drill on that wave risks all of that. The ego says: don't waste a good wave on a drill.

I have watched this happen with Bryan Pérez in preparation for the Olympics. With national champions. With surfers who intellectually understood everything in this chapter and still, on a good wave, defaulted to performance rather than training.

Recognizing this pattern in yourself is most of the work. Once you see it, you can choose differently. Once you see the ego's interference for what it is — a mechanism of stagnation dressed as competitive instinct — you can thank it and continue with your objective.

What a Training Session Actually Looks Like

A Foundation-level surfer. Two-to-three-foot beach break. The objective: stay close to the pocket through the early part of the wave.

Five waves. On four of them, the objective was missed — paddling too far down the line, losing the pocket, surfing the face instead of the power source. On one of them, the positioning was right. Not a great wave. A correct execution.

After the session: one question. Did I work on what I came to work on? Not: did I surf well? Not: were the waves good? Did I work on what I came to work on?

If yes — even if the execution was imperfect, even if only one out of five waves hit the target — the session was a training session. Something was built.

If no — if the objective disappeared after the first set and the session became free surfing — then the session was enjoyable, but it was not training. Nothing new was built.

Both are valid choices. The only mistake is confusing one for the other.

PART II

THE FRICTION

Growth requires discomfort. This section is about learning to breathe inside of it.

Chapter 3

The Comfort Zone: Why Growth Feels Like Regression

In 2017, I started training Brazilian jiu-jitsu.

I came to it as a competitive athlete with decades of experience. I understood how to learn. I understood training methodology. I had coached others through difficult developmental phases. None of that prepared me for what happened in my second year of training.

I was working on a specific positional detail — a small adjustment in a guard position that my instructor had identified as a foundational gap. The adjustment was subtle. But integrating it required me to dismantle something automatic and rebuild it consciously. And for weeks, everything fell apart.

People who had never submitted me started winning. Training partners I had been dominating regularly were suddenly competitive. My timing broke. My reads slowed. I felt, objectively, worse at jiu-jitsu than I had been before I started working on this specific thing.

John Danaher — widely considered the greatest jiu-jitsu coach in the world — has a way of describing this that I have never found a better version of: "The price of evolution is temporarily abandoning your comfort zone with the certainty that the regression you feel is part of the process."

Temporarily. With certainty. Part of the process.

Not a sign that something has gone wrong. A sign that something is being built.

---

What Is Actually Happening

When you introduce a new skill or movement pattern, you are asking the nervous system to do something it doesn't yet know how to do automatically. The brain has to process it consciously — slowly, effortfully, in real time — while simultaneously managing everything else required to surf.

Reading the wave. Timing the pop-up. Managing balance. Adjusting to changing water. All of that is still happening. And now there is also this new conscious task competing for processing power.

The result is predictable: everything gets slightly worse. The timing breaks. The reads slow. Waves that felt manageable before now feel fast. The surfer feels less competent, not more.

This is not getting worse at surfing. It is getting temporarily worse at the automatic version of surfing — while building something new that will eventually become automatic itself. The awkwardness is not failure. It is evidence of change.

The Breaking Point

This is where most surfers stop.

Not the beginners. Beginners expect to struggle — everything is new, and there is no existing self-image to protect. The surfers who are most vulnerable to stopping at this point are the ones who have reached a level of genuine competence. Who can have good sessions. Who know what surfing well feels like.

For those surfers, the next level requires temporarily leaving behind the comfortable version of themselves that they have built. It requires accepting, for a period of time, feeling less capable than they know themselves to be.

The ego's response to this is predictable: don't risk it. Stay where you are competent. Free surf the good waves instead of working on the drill. Protect the self-image.

Most surfers comply — not consciously, but because nobody explained that the discomfort they're feeling is a signal that something is working. They interpret it as failure. They back off. And the next level remains permanently out of reach.

Frustration Is Information

Frustration in the learning process is not a verdict on your ability. It is information.

Specifically, it is information that the nervous system is being asked to do something it cannot yet do automatically. That gap — between where you are and what you're attempting — is exactly the gap where growth happens. Without the gap, there is no stimulus for adaptation. Without the discomfort, there is no signal that something new is being built.

The surfer who never feels frustrated is the surfer who is not pushing beyond the automatic. They are reinforcing what's already there. They may be having great sessions. They are not improving.

The clumsiness, the awkwardness, the sense of regression — these are evidence of change, not failure. Learning to read them correctly changes everything about how you move through the difficult phases of development.

The Learning Zone

There is a specific zone of challenge that produces optimal learning. It is not the zone where everything is comfortable and automatic. It is not the zone where the challenge is so overwhelming that the nervous system shuts down into survival mode.

It is the zone just beyond current automatic capacity. Where the challenge is real enough to require full attention, where you will miss some attempts and succeed enough others to understand what the target feels like.

Beginners often stumble into this zone naturally — everything is beyond their current capacity, so almost any challenge is appropriately stretching. Advanced surfers have to be more deliberate about finding it, because their automatic capacity is high enough that many situations don't push them.

The learning zone requires two things: an honest assessment of where your automatic capacity actually is, and the willingness to work in the place that feels slightly worse than your best. Not your worst. Not overwhelming. Just beyond comfortable.

Beginner's Mind

There is a concept in Zen philosophy — shoshin, or "beginner's mind" — that describes the willingness to approach something with openness and lack of preconception, even as an expert.

In surfing terms, it is the willingness to try something new without already knowing how it's going to go. To approach a drill with genuine curiosity rather than the defensive crouch of someone protecting their self-image.

"I already know" closes the door to new information. "Let me find out what happens" leaves it open. The surfer who approaches every session with something of a beginner's mind — regardless of their level — learns faster than the one who is defending their existing identity.

This does not mean forgetting what you know. It means holding it lightly enough to let it be refined.

Chapter 4

Entering Flow: Where Presence Becomes Performance

Las Flores, El Salvador. Late afternoon light. Waves coming through with regularity — not perfect, but consistent and good.

I remember paddling into a wave and something shifting. Not dramatically — there was no moment of revelation. But somewhere in the middle of that session, the gap between intention and execution disappeared. I wasn't thinking about what I was doing. I was doing it. The board felt like an extension of my body rather than an object I was managing. Time synchronized — not slowed down or sped up, but accurate in a way it usually isn't. I was completely there.

Board, wave, wind, body, mind — all of it one thing.

I've had this experience enough times now to recognize it when it starts, and to understand something about the conditions that make it more likely. But I've also been in perfect waves, on my best board, in ideal conditions, and felt none of it — grinding through a session where nothing connected, where every wave felt like a negotiation.

The difference was not the ocean. It was something internal. Something about the state I was in when I paddled out.

---

What Flow Actually Is

Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi, the psychologist who gave flow its name and spent decades studying it, described it as complete immersion in an activity — energized focus, full involvement, and a sense of effortlessness despite the difficulty of what you're doing.

Neuroscientifically, flow involves a specific shift in how the brain is using its resources. The prefrontal cortex — the part responsible for self-monitoring, self-evaluation, and conscious control — becomes less active. The motor systems, sensory processing, and pattern recognition systems take over. You stop thinking about doing and start doing. The friction of self-consciousness disappears.

This is why things that require conscious thought during normal practice become automatic in flow. Why timing that usually requires calculation becomes instinctive. Why the body seems to know what to do before the mind has caught up.

The Flow Channel

Flow does not happen in all conditions. It requires a specific relationship between challenge and capability.

Imagine a diagonal channel running between two walls. The wall on one side is anxiety: when the challenge significantly exceeds your capability, the body tightens, the mind races, and performance collapses. The wall on the other side is boredom: when your capability significantly exceeds the challenge, the mind wanders, motivation drops, and performance becomes mechanical.

Flow lives in the channel between those two walls. The challenge is real and stretching — it requires your full capability. But your capability is sufficient to meet it with full engagement. You are neither overwhelmed nor unchallenged.

This channel moves as you improve. What was challenging at Foundation level becomes automatic at Emerging level. The flow channel calibrates to your current capability — which means finding it requires an honest understanding of where you actually are.

Bryan Pérez at the World Championship

At a World Surfing Championship, Bryan Pérez faced Jeremy Flores — a current Championship Tour surfer, one of the best in the world.

I watched the heat from the beach. What I could see from that distance was alignment. Bryan was surfing at the very top of his capability — the challenge was high, perhaps the highest he had ever faced in competition. But he was not overwhelmed. He was not playing it safe. He was fully present in each moment of each wave.

What I was watching was not relaxation, and it was not aggression. It was alignment. Challenge and capability meeting in the channel where flow lives.

He performed the best surfing of his life in that heat — not despite the pressure, but inside it. Because the pressure calibrated the challenge to exactly the level that required everything he had.

How Flow Breaks

Flow is interrupted by four things, and all four involve a specific shift in attention away from the present moment:

Comparison — measuring yourself against others in the lineup, calculating where you stand relative to competitors or peers. The moment this calculation begins, you are no longer surfing. You are managing an image.

Outcome focus — checking how the session is going rather than attending to what you're doing. "Is this working?" "Am I improving?" These questions pull attention away from execution and into evaluation.

Mismatched challenge — conditions that are too easy (mind wanders) or too hard (mind tightens). Neither end of the spectrum supports flow.

Unclear intention — when there is no specific focus, the mind fills the vacuum with whatever is loudest: worry, comparison, judgment, the memory of the last mistake.

Each of these is an attention problem. Flow breaks when attention moves from the present execution to something else. And attention moves when there isn't something specific enough to hold it.

Building the Conditions for Flow

Flow cannot be forced. But the conditions that make it more likely can be built deliberately.

Right challenge for your level. A board appropriate for the conditions. A spot that offers waves your capability can engage with fully. A clear intention — not a rigid objective, but a specific focus that gives the attention something to track.

And before paddling out: the breath. The deliberate reset. The choice to be here now rather than carrying yesterday's frustrations or tomorrow's concerns into the water.

None of this guarantees flow. But it changes the probability. And over hundreds of sessions, probability becomes pattern.

Chapter 5

Setting Expectations: The Mental Hack That Unlocks Enjoyment

I was in Indonesia. The trip I had been building toward for years — the destination I had imagined in detail, the waves I had seen in videos since I was a teenager. Everything aligned: the swell, the wind, the time, the people I was traveling with.

And I was not flowing.

I knew it from the first session. Something was off — not in the conditions, but in me. A tightness. A gap between what I was expecting and what I was experiencing. I was surfing, but I was surfing against a standard I had constructed before I ever got in the water, and the reality — even though it was genuinely good — couldn't match the image I had been carrying for years.

I recognized what was happening because I've seen it in athletes I've coached. I knew the mechanism. Knowing it didn't make it stop.

So I did something deliberate. I talked to one of the athletes traveling with me. I said it out loud: I'm fighting my own expectations. And then I chose a mantra — not as inspiration, but as a strategic adjustment. Three words I could return to every time the frustration appeared:

Expect nothing. Enjoy everything.

Not a philosophy. A tool.

Every time the familiar tightness appeared — the gap between expectation and reality — I repeated it. Let go of the image. Be in this water, on this wave, right now.

The ocean didn't change. The internal pressure changed. And when it changed, the timing returned. The flow returned. The barrels came.

The sea didn't change. I did.

---

The Most Destructive Expectation

The belief that surfing should feel good all the time is the expectation that causes the most damage.

When a surfer holds this belief — and most surfers hold it implicitly, without ever examining it — every uncomfortable session becomes evidence of a problem. Every awkward phase is interpreted as failure. Every session where the flow doesn't come is a verdict.

Surfing stops being an experience. It becomes a performance — every session an evaluation, every wave a test, every exit from the water a verdict on your worth as a surfer.

This is not a sustainable relationship with the sport. It cannot survive what real improvement requires: the willingness to go through phases where nothing works, where everything feels worse than before, where the gap between your current state and your desired state is not closing but widening.

The French Waterman

He was in his early forties. He had come to surfing as a complete beginner and trained seriously for two years. At Foundation level, he had real skills. And then everything fell apart.

He came to a session angry. Not at the waves, not at external circumstances — at himself, at his surfing, at the process. "I'm surfing worse than I was before. I didn't start this to be angry. I want to quit."

I took him to the progression map on the wall of the academy and read the Foundation level description aloud. The complexity increasing. The foundations being tested. The specific frustration of almost but not quite. I asked him: "Is this what you're experiencing?"

He was quiet for a moment. "Yes. Exactly."

"Then you are exactly where you're supposed to be."

I watched his face change. The tension of fighting his own experience released. He hadn't been failing. He had been accurately experiencing what Foundation level feels like from the inside — and interpreting it as something wrong with him, rather than as evidence that he was precisely where the map said he should be.

He is now approaching Emerging level. He still has hard sessions. He no longer interprets them as failures.

The Mechanism of Frustration

Frustration is not a response to difficulty. Difficulty is manageable — we are built to meet challenges. Frustration is a response to the gap between expectation and reality.

When the gap is small — when you expected something to be hard and it is hard — the experience is challenging but coherent. You can work with it.

When the gap is large — when you expected progress to feel like progress and it feels like regression — the experience is disorienting. The mind casts around for an explanation and usually lands on the wrong one: something is wrong with me.

Almost always, the expectation is wrong. Not you.

Two Timelines

Surf development happens on two parallel timelines that don't always move together.

The first is technical performance: what your body does on the wave. Pop-up, stance, turns, maneuvers. This is the visible timeline — the one most surfers track, the one that's easiest to measure.

The second is ocean knowledge: reading conditions, wave selection, positioning, understanding energy. This timeline is less visible and develops on its own schedule, influenced by time in the water, observation, and experience that often doesn't register as progress in the moment.

A surfer can improve significantly on the ocean knowledge timeline while feeling stagnant on the technical timeline — and vice versa. Tracking only one misses half the story and produces frustration that has no accurate target.

What to Do With a Wipeout

On the first day of every beginner course I teach, I tell the students this:

Surfing is sunsets and adrenaline and the feeling of a wave moving under you and the specific joy of sliding across a face of water. And surfing is also wipeouts and getting held under and saltwater in places it shouldn't be and coming up after a two-wave hold-down with burning lungs and sandy board shorts.

Both of those things are surfing. Not the first one without the second. Both.

The best surfer in the world gets worked regularly. Not occasionally — regularly. Wipeouts are a permanent feature of the sport, not a temporary inconvenience on the way to mastery.

When a wave takes you down, you have two options. You can fight the hold-down — resist the water, try to surface immediately, come up frustrated and take the experience personally. Or you can let it happen. Stop fighting the water. Let it move through you. Come up, breathe, get back on the board, keep going.

This is a literal choice every time the ocean takes you down. It is also a precise description of how to meet every difficult moment in the learning process — the awkward phase, the session where nothing works, the expectation colliding with reality.

The ocean is not going to change. Your relationship to what it offers — including what knocks you down — is entirely yours to choose.

---

Where Are You Right Now?

Knowing your level is not about labeling yourself. It is about understanding what the terrain you are currently navigating is supposed to feel like — so you can stop fighting your own experience and start working with it.

Read through the following descriptions honestly. Not with the level you wish you were at. With the level that actually matches what happens when you are in the water.

🟢 Beginner

You are learning that the ocean is alive and that the board is not.

Everything is new. The pop-up, the balance, the timing — none of it is automatic yet. You fall more than you stand. You miss more waves than you catch. The ocean feels large and unpredictable.

This is exactly right.

What's normal here: exhaustion after short sessions, inconsistency on the pop-up, difficulty reading basic wave direction, feeling like you're starting over every time you paddle out.

What progress looks like: slightly more time on your feet. A growing sense that the ocean is readable, even if you can't fully read it yet.

🟡 Novice

You can stand up. Now you're learning what to do once you're standing.

The pop-up is becoming more reliable. You're catching small green waves. But consistency is elusive — some sessions feel like breakthroughs, others feel like you've forgotten everything.

This is exactly right.

What's normal here: good days followed by confusing days, timing that works until it doesn't.

What progress looks like: the gap between your best and average slowly narrowing.

🔵 Foundation

You have real skills. Now the gaps are becoming visible.

This is where the French Waterman wanted to quit. You can surf. But the complexity is increasing, and the foundations that were sufficient for easier conditions are being tested by harder ones.

This is exactly right.

What's normal here: bottom turns that worked last month suddenly failing, timing that breaks in new conditions.

What progress looks like: increasing consistency in specific skills. The ability to identify exactly what went wrong.

🟣 Emerging

You're building a real game. And dismantling it at the same time.

You're exploring more advanced maneuvers. Your style is developing. And with every new piece you add, something else temporarily breaks.

This is exactly right.

What's normal here: sessions that feel like regression, temporary loss of confidence.

What progress looks like: maneuvers that were impossible becoming possible. A growing sense of your own style.

🟤 Pre-Elite

The technique is there. The mental game is the frontier.

The limiting factor is rarely physical or technical now. It is psychological. Pressure management, consistency under competition, the ability to perform when it matters.

This is exactly right.

What's normal here: performing well in practice and tightening in competition, knowing what to do but not doing it when stakes are real.

What progress looks like: the gap between practice and competition performance narrowing.

⚫ Elite

You are not chasing the next level. You are refining this one.

Mastery is about precision, consistency, performing at the highest level across all conditions. The work is subtle. The margins are small.

This is exactly right.

What's normal here: not every session being peak performance, careful management of energy, the challenge of staying motivated when external markers have slowed.

What progress looks like: longevity. Consistency over time.

Find your level. Not the one you want. The one that matches what actually happens when you paddle out. That is your starting point. And that starting point is exactly where you're supposed to be.

Chapter 6

How to Build a Training Plan That Actually Works

There is a specific thing I've watched happen with surfers at every level, and it is always recognizable even from the beach.

It is the moment when the prize changes.

Before structure, the prize is the wave. The perfect ride, the clean maneuver, the session that felt good. The surfer enters the water hoping for something — a result, a feeling, a confirmation that they are as good as they think they are or getting closer to where they want to be. The wave is the reward. And when the wave doesn't deliver it, the session feels like a loss.

After structure, something shifts internally. The prize is no longer the wave. The prize is executing the objective.

A surfer with a real plan can have a technically average session — small waves, inconsistent conditions, nothing impressive — and walk out of the water with a genuine sense of accomplishment. Because they came with a specific mission, and they completed it. The wave was the vehicle. The objective was the destination.

That shift — from outcome to performance, from result to execution — is the moment a surfer stops being someone who surfs and starts being someone who trains.

---

The Four Pillars of a Real Plan

Here is the mistake most surfers make when they try to build their own training plan: they build a technical plan.

They identify a maneuver they want to improve, design sessions around it, and track whether it's getting better. That is a start. But it is incomplete — because surfing is not only technical, and a plan that only addresses technique will always have a ceiling.

A complete training plan works across four areas simultaneously. Not equally in every session, but all four present in any given week.

Physical. Strength, endurance, mobility, the capacity to perform at the end of a session the way you performed at the beginning. This is the engine. If the engine is undertrained, everything built on top of it has limits.

Technical. What your body does on the wave. Foot placement, timing, compression, line. The mechanics of surfing.

Tactical. How you read the ocean. Wave selection, positioning, decision-making under pressure, adapting to conditions. This is the intelligence layer.

Mental. Focus, expectation management, resilience. Everything the previous chapters of this book have been about. Not a soft addition — a structural component. A plan without it is missing the layer that determines whether everything else works when it matters.

Assess yourself honestly across all four. Find the weakest pillar. That is usually where the most available progress is hiding.

Goals That Actually Work

Big goals without structure create anxiety. Small goals without vision create boredom. You need both, connected.

Long-term goals give you direction — what kind of surfer do I want to become? These live months or years away.

Medium-term goals give you stages — reaching Foundation level, improving competition consistency. Weeks to months away.

Short-term goals give you momentum — what you work on this week, in this session, on this wave.

The mistake most surfers make is working only at one level. Long-term goals without short-term actions are dreams. Short-term actions without long-term goals are busywork. The plan that works connects all three.

What a Real Week Looks Like

A well-designed training week has a simple logic: you build, you rest, you test, you evaluate. Everything else is detail.

The week starts with diagnosis — entering the water without pressure, feeling where you are, then narrowing to one specific objective. Not the whole surf. One thing. You work that thing with full attention for enough sessions to actually move it.

Midweek you rest. Not because you're tired — because adaptation happens during recovery, not during training. The body builds the skill while you sleep, not while you surf. Remove the rest and the week becomes a grind. Keep it and the week becomes a system.

Toward the end of the week you test what you've been building — first in low pressure, then with something at stake. A timer. A wave count. Anything that introduces the kind of pressure that reveals whether the skill is real or only works when nothing matters.

The week closes with one honest question: did anything move? Not dramatically. Measurably. Even slightly. If yes, build on it next week. If no, understand why before changing the objective.

Build. Rest. Test. Evaluate. Repeat.

What goes inside that structure changes based on your level and what the ocean gives you. But the logic stays the same whether you're a beginner working on your pop-up or an Olympic athlete preparing for competition.

The Plan Is a Living Document

A training plan is not a contract. It is a map — and maps get updated when the terrain turns out to be different from what you expected.

Conditions change. Life changes. Some weeks the objective clicks faster than expected. Some weeks require more repetition. A good plan bends without breaking. It has enough structure to provide direction and enough flexibility to survive contact with reality.

What it never does is disappear entirely. A surfer without any plan is just hoping. A surfer with a plan — even an imperfect one that needs constant adjustment — is building something.

Chapter 7

The Law of Action: From Understanding to Doing

There is a specific thing I hear from surfers more than almost anything else. It sounds reasonable. It sounds even wise, in a certain light.

Surfing is natural. You don't train surfing.

Sometimes it comes with a companion: I only surf for fun. Or the quietest version, the one that's hardest to argue with: I'm fine where I am.

These are not wrong things to want. Surfing for joy, surfing without pressure, being content with your current level — all of that is legitimate. The ocean doesn't owe anyone a progression plan. Not everyone needs to optimize.

But here is what I've learned after years of hearing these phrases:

Most of the time, they are not expressions of contentment. They are expressions of fear.

Not always. But often enough that it's worth naming directly.

The fear of discovering you're not as good as you believed. The fear of losing the flow you've built while working through an awkward phase. The fear of feeling like a beginner again — of exposing the gaps, of being seen struggling with something you think you should already have.

When someone says I don't train, what they are sometimes really saying is: I don't want to leave the place where I feel competent.

That is a completely human response. There is no judgment in naming it. But there is a difference between choosing not to train and lying to yourself about why.

Honesty about the why is everything.

---

The Gap Between Understanding and Action

We live in a time when information is unlimited. You can learn the biomechanics of a bottom turn, the neuroscience of flow, the psychology of expectation management — all of it available, all of it accessible, none of it scarce.

And yet surfers plateau. They read, they understand, they agree — and then they paddle out and do exactly what they have always done.

This is not a failure of intelligence. It is a failure of activation.

The nervous system does not adapt to understanding. It adapts to repetition. The brain does not rewire itself because you read something compelling. It rewires itself because you attempted something, failed, adjusted, and attempted again — enough times, with enough attention, for a new pattern to consolidate.

You can understand the entire system in this book and improve nothing.

Or you can take one idea from one chapter, carry it into the water tomorrow, and begin building something real.

The difference between those two outcomes is not comprehension. It is action. Specifically: the first action, taken before the motivation fades and the understanding settles back into theory.

The Ritual Is Not Optional

I have ADHD. My mind does not naturally quiet itself. Ideas arrive constantly — projects, systems, training concepts, things to build, things to fix. Without structure, that mental energy scatters. It goes everywhere and lands nowhere.

The daily ritual — breathing, mobility, connection, silence, intention — is not something I do because it optimizes performance metrics. I do it because without it, I am a different person.

Not dramatically different. But recognizably different.

Without the ritual, I operate from reaction. I respond to whatever is loudest rather than choosing what matters. I lose the thread of what I'm building.

With the ritual, I operate from intention. The day has a center. The session has a purpose.

I understood this not through a single dramatic moment but through repetition. Through noticing, over weeks and months, the consistent difference between my aligned version and my scattered version.

The ritual doesn't improve my surfing directly. It improves me. And when I improve, everything I do improves with it.

A machine needs oil to run. A mind needs alignment to perform. The ritual is not a luxury for people with extra time. It is maintenance for the instrument you use to do everything else.

How Much Do You Actually Want It?

There is a question underneath everything in this book, and I want to ask it directly before we finish.

How much do you actually want this?

Not in the abstract. Not as an image of yourself surfing better at some future point. Right now, with full awareness of what real improvement requires — the uncomfortable sessions, the phases where you feel worse before you feel better, the repetition that doesn't always feel like progress, the patience that has to outlast the frustration.

Knowing all of that: how much do you want it?

If the answer is genuinely, deeply yes — then the path is clear. Accept what it requires. Enter the water with intention. Build the plan. Do the work. Trust the process even when it doesn't feel like progress.

If the answer is that you want surfing to be joyful and free and connected to nature without the structure of deliberate training — that is also a complete answer. Not every surfer is called to optimize. Not every session needs to be a training session.

But decide consciously. Know which one you're choosing and why.

Real motivation appears when you know why you do what you do. For yourself. For the sensation. For growth. For competition. For healing. For play. When the reason is real, action follows naturally. When it isn't, no system in the world will move you.

One Wave. One Intention. Starting Now.

Here is what I want you to do before you put this book down.

Not a plan. Not a commitment to a new training program. Not a promise to yourself about who you're going to become.

One thing.

Decide what your focus is for your next session. One specific thing — something from this book, something you've been wanting to work on, something small enough to actually hold in your attention for an entire surf. Write it down if you need to. Say it out loud if that helps. But make it concrete, make it specific, and make it yours.

That is the entire assignment.

Because the surfer who paddles out tomorrow with one clear intention is already different from the surfer who paddled out yesterday with none.

The system in this book works. Not because it is sophisticated — it isn't. Because it is honest about how learning actually happens, and it gives you the tools to work with that reality instead of against it.

But the system only works if you use it.

The intelligent surfer understands that.

The wise surfer paddles out.

Final Words

The Journey Is the Point

I'm not talking to you as a coach right now. Not as the author of this book.

I'm talking to you as a surfer.

The sooner you understand that surfing is a journey — not a destination, not a level, not a maneuver, not a ranking — the sooner you'll start enjoying it for real.

Every missed wave. Every magical session. Every frustrating morning when nothing worked. Every afternoon when everything did.

That is the surf. All of it.

When you stop running toward some imagined finish line and start being present on the path, something shifts. You celebrate small wins. You breathe differently. You connect more deeply with the ocean. You stop measuring every session against an impossible standard and start finding value in what's actually there.

This book gave you tools. Structure. Philosophy. A system for turning time in the water into real progress.

But if I had to reduce everything to one thing, it would be this:

Enjoy the journey.

It's yours. Nobody else's. Nobody can take the peace from it. Nobody can take the joy. Nobody can take the connection you build with the ocean over years of showing up, session after session, wave after wave.

Be yourself. Surf your process.

One wave. One intention. One session at a time.

Let's go.

— Marcelo Castellanos
