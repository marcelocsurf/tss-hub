SECTION 1: HOW THE SURF SEQUENCE® VIEWS SAFETY

Safety is not there to slow you down. It is there to help you learn faster, with better decisions, better awareness, and more consistent progress.

Your coach is not only teaching technique. Your coach is also reading the ocean, the group, your energy, and the conditions around you. Safety is built into every session — from the moment the venue is analyzed to the moment the last student exits the water.

This manual gives you everything you need to understand your role in keeping sessions safe, productive, and enjoyable. Read it before your first session. Review it whenever you have questions.

SECTION 2: YOUR RESPONSIBILITIES AS A STUDENT

Safety is shared. The coach leads, but you have responsibilities too. These are not optional — they are part of how the method works.

  • Tell the truth. Report injuries, fear, fatigue, medication, panic history, or anything that could affect your session. Your coach needs accurate information to make good decisions.
  • Follow commands. Once the session is active, follow the coach’s commands quickly and without debate. There will be time for questions before entry — not during a critical moment in the water.
  • Respect your level. Do not jump exposure just to prove something. Progression is earned through control, not ambition.
  • Control your board. Your board is a projectile. Do not let it become a danger to yourself or others. Hold onto it whenever possible.
  • Respect the ocean. Respect the beach, the people surfing around you, and the environment. Leave the beach cleaner than you found it.
  • Respect other surfers. Do not create danger through board loss, cutting across entry lines, or ignoring assigned zones. Conflict is never your job to manage — the coach handles it.

SECTION 3: BEFORE ENTERING THE WATER

Every session begins with preparation. Do not skip this — it is where safety starts.

The Beach Briefing

Before every session, your coach will deliver a beach briefing. This is not a formality. Pay attention.

  • Session objective: What you will work on today and what success looks like.
  • Where to enter: The exact route into the water.
  • Where to stay: Your assigned zone — the area between the flags or markers.
  • Where NOT to go: The no-go zone, usually the impact zone where bigger waves break.
  • Where to exit: The agreed route back to shore.
  • Recall signal: What it sounds or looks like, and what to do when you hear or see it. When the recall signal is given, return to shore immediately through the agreed route.
  • What to do if: Your board is lost, if fear spikes, or if the current pulls you sideways.

Equipment Check

  • Check your leash — is it attached securely to your ankle and to the board?
  • Check your board — are the fins secure? Is the surface intact?
  • Check your wetsuit or rash vest — does it fit properly?
  • Apply sunscreen and hydrate before entry.

SECTION 4: IN THE WATER

Once you are in the water, these rules protect you, your group, and your learning quality.

  • Stay with the group. Remain inside the assigned zone at all times. Do not drift outside the flagged area.
  • Hold your board. Whenever possible, maintain contact with your board. During falls, protect your head first, then recover your board.
  • Protect your head. After every wipeout, cover your head with your arms before surfacing. This is non-negotiable.
  • Respect wave assignments. Do not paddle into waves, sections, or areas your coach has not approved for your level.
  • Stay calm. If you lose control, drift out of zone, or get scared, focus on staying calm and following one instruction at a time. Panic increases danger. Breathing reduces it.

Spacing and Awareness

Keep enough distance from other surfers to avoid board collisions. Be aware of who is around you, especially behind you. If someone is paddling for a wave and has priority, let them go. Surf etiquette is collision prevention.

SECTION 5: WHAT TO DO IF...

Situations will arise. Knowing what to do before they happen is what separates a prepared surfer from a panicked one.

You lose your board

  • Protect your head immediately — arms crossed over your head.
  • Swim to shore if your leash breaks.
  • Alert your coach as soon as possible.
  • Do NOT chase a loose board into deeper water or the impact zone.

You feel scared or panicked

  • Signal the coach — raise one arm.
  • Float on your back and focus on breathing.
  • Do not fight the water. Let it carry you while you regain composure.
  • If you cannot calm down, the coach will come to you. Trust the process.

You drift out of your zone

  • Paddle parallel to shore — not against the current.
  • If you cannot get back, signal the coach and stay calm.
  • Never exhaust yourself fighting a current. Use the current, do not fight it.

You hear the recall signal

  • Return to shore immediately through the agreed exit route.
  • Do not catch one more wave. The signal means NOW.
  • Regroup at the meeting point and wait for instructions.

Someone else is in trouble

  • Alert the coach immediately — point and shout.
  • Do NOT attempt a rescue unless you are trained. A second person in trouble doubles the emergency.
  • Stay visible and stay safe so you do not become a second victim.

You get hit by a board

  • Check yourself: are you bleeding? Is your vision clear? Do you feel dizzy?
  • If you hit your head, exit the water immediately — even if you feel fine.
  • Report ALL impacts to your coach. Head injuries can have delayed symptoms.

SECTION 6: SURF ETIQUETTE AS SAFETY

Etiquette is not just good manners. In the ocean, etiquette is collision prevention, social de-escalation, and line-up order. Breaking etiquette creates real danger.

  • Priority: The surfer closest to the peak (the breaking part of the wave) has priority. If someone is already riding, do not drop in.
  • Paddle wide: When paddling back out, go around the breaking zone — not through it. Paddling through the impact zone puts you in the path of riders.
  • Control your board: Never throw your board. If a wave is coming, hold onto it or turtle roll. A loose board is a projectile.
  • Respect locals: Every beach has regular surfers. Be respectful, do not take every wave, and do not paddle into the middle of their lineup.
  • Communicate: If you are about to collide, shout to alert the other person. Simple communication prevents many accidents.

SECTION 7: UNDERSTANDING CONDITIONS

As you progress in The Surf Sequence®, you will develop the ability to read conditions yourself. For now, understand the basics of what your coach is evaluating before every session.

What Your Coach Reads

  • Swell size: The height and power of the waves. Bigger swell = more force, more risk.
  • Tide: The water level changes throughout the day. Some breaks are safer at mid-tide. Your coach selects the right time.
  • Current: Water moving sideways or pulling outward. Current can drag you out of your zone without you noticing.
  • Wind: Offshore wind (from land to ocean) cleans up the waves. Onshore wind (from ocean to land) makes conditions messier and harder to read.
  • Bottom: Sand, reef, or rocks underneath. Your coach selects venues with appropriate bottom conditions for your level.
  • Crowd: More surfers = more collision risk. Your coach adjusts the zone and spacing based on crowd.

Condition Colors

The Surf Sequence® uses a color-coded condition system to classify safety levels. Your coach will always operate within these thresholds:

  • Green — Very Safe / Safe: 1–4 ft swell. Low current. Standard ratios. All levels can enter.
  • Yellow — Challenging: 5–6 ft swell. Moderate to strong current. Tighter ratios. Age and level restrictions apply.
  • Orange — Strong / Heavy: 7–10 ft swell. 1:1 ratio mandatory. No beginners. No children under 14.
  • Red — RED CODE: 10+ ft or any environmental hazard. CLASS CANCELLED. No one enters the water. Plan B is activated.

SECTION 9: OCEAN RESPECT & ENVIRONMENTAL SAFETY

Respect for the ocean and the beach is part of safe delivery and part of being a surfer. The ocean is powerful, beautiful, and deserves care.

  • Leave the beach cleaner than you found it. Pick up trash — even if it is not yours.
  • Do not damage marine life or coral. Be aware of where you step and where your board rests.
  • Respect local access norms and community rules.
  • Handle equipment carefully. Damaged gear left on the beach creates hazards for others.
  • Report any environmental hazard you observe (pollution, debris, dead animals) to your coach.

Safety is not fear. It is applied intelligence.

Core Principle In The Surf Sequence®, safety means preparation, observation, correct progression, emotional regulation, equipment discipline, group control, and timely adaptation. A safe session is not the opposite of a challenging session. It is the condition that makes real challenge usable.
Pro Tip Ask questions BEFORE entry, not during a critical moment in the water. If anything is unclear after the briefing, raise your hand and ask. Once you enter the water, communication becomes harder.
Falling Safely When you fall, protect your head with both arms crossed over it. Fall flat (pencil jump or starfish), not feet-first into shallow water. After surfacing, locate your board immediately and regain control of it.
Head Injury Rule Any head impact means you are OUT of the water for the rest of the session. No exceptions. This is not punishment — it is protection. Delayed concussion symptoms are invisible but dangerous.
Remember Conflict in the water is never your job to manage. If a local surfer or another person becomes aggressive, move away and alert your coach. Your coach will handle it.
Red Code Hazards Jellyfish, stingrays, exposed rocks, sharks, crocodiles, contaminated water, boat traffic, venomous creatures, lightning, or any condition that threatens student or instructor safety — any of these triggers Red Code regardless of swell size.
