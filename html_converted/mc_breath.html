NOSE vs MOUTH — THE FUNDAMENTAL CHOICE

NASAL BREATHING
  • Filters, warms, humidifies air
  • Activates diaphragm efficiently
  • Produces nitric oxide (6x more)
  • Improves oxygen absorption
  • Slows breathing rate naturally
  • Increases HRV (stress resilience)
  • Enhances endurance during exercise
  • Supports deep sleep quality
MOUTH BREATHING
  • No filtration — raw air to lungs
  • Chest breathing (shallow)
  • Minimal nitric oxide
  • Poor oxygen exchange
  • Faster, inefficient breathing
  • Lowers HRV (more stress)
  • Exhausts faster during exercise
  • Disrupts sleep (apnea, snoring)
  • Nestor’s Stanford experiment: 10 days mouth-only breathing → blood pressure spiked, sleep apnea appeared, stress markers surged. All reversed within days of nasal breathing.

CO₂ — THE MISUNDERSTOOD MOLECULE

CO₂ is NOT just a waste product. The Bohr Effect: Carbon dioxide is required for hemoglobin to release oxygen into your tissues. Without adequate CO₂, your blood carries oxygen but can’t deliver it. You’re oxygenated but starving. CO₂ tolerance is trainable. Elite athletes like freedivers and climbers have trained their chemoreceptors to handle strong CO₂ fluctuations without triggering panic. The same training applies to surfers during hold-downs. “Carbon dioxide is, in fact, a more fundamental component of living matter than is oxygen.” — Yandell Henderson, Yale Physiologist

WHY BREATH MATTERS FOR SURFING

Paddle endurance
  • Nasal breathing during paddling cuts exertion nearly in half (Douillard, cited by Nestor). You arrive at the peak less exhausted.
Hold-down tolerance
  • CO₂ tolerance training directly extends how long you stay calm underwater. The panic is in the CO₂ sensitivity, not the oxygen.
Wave reading
  • Slow breathing expands peripheral awareness. Mouth breathing creates tunnel vision. You read the wave better when breathing slowly.
Pre-heat calm
  • 5.5-second coherent breathing before a competition heat activates parasympathetic response. Heart rate drops. Focus sharpens.
Recovery between waves
  • A single long exhale while sitting on your board resets your nervous system between sets.
Sleep and learning
  • Nasal breathing during sleep improves recovery and learning consolidation. Tape your mouth at night (Nestor’s recommendation).

5 BREATHING TECHNIQUES FOR SURFERS

01

Coherent Breathing
5.5 sec in, 5.5 sec out. The universal rhythm. Use daily and before sessions.

02

Box Breathing

4-4-4-4. Inhale, hold, exhale, hold. Used by Navy SEALs. Maximum focus under pressure.

03

4-7-8 Method

Inhale 4, hold 7, exhale 8. Activates vagus nerve. Fastest parasympathetic trigger.

04

Nasal-Only Training

Breathe only through nose during warm-up and low-intensity paddling. Builds efficiency.

05

CO₂ Tolerance Walk

Walk normally, exhale + pinch nose, hold until air hunger, release and breathe gently. Extends hold-down capacity.

CO₂ TOLERANCE TRAINING FOR SURFERS

This is what separates surfers who panic during hold-downs from surfers who stay calm. The difference is not lung size — it’s CO₂ sensitivity.

1
Walk or jog
Breathe normally through your nose for 60 seconds.
2
Exhale normally
Then pinch your nose closed. Keep walking at the same pace.
3
Hold until air hunger
Not until desperation — just until you feel the urge to breathe.
4
Release and recover
Breathe very gently, about half your normal volume, for 10–15 seconds.
5
Repeat
3–5 rounds. Gradually extend the hold time over weeks.
  • From Nestor: “Simply training nasal breathing could cut total exertion in half and offer huge gains in endurance.” — Dr. John Douillard

HOLD-DOWN BREATHING PROTOCOL

What to do when the wave pushes you under.

BEFORE the set arrives
Take 3 deep breaths through the nose. Fill the belly. Don’t hyperventilate.
As the wave hits
Exhale slightly to reduce buoyancy. Cover your head. Relax your body.
Underwater

Do NOT fight. Relax every muscle. The wave will release you. Count slowly: 1…2…3…

The urge to breathe hits
This is CO₂, not lack of oxygen. You have more time than you think. Stay calm.
Surface
One strong exhale to clear airways. Then slow nasal inhale. Do NOT gasp.
Recovery
3 cycles of coherent breathing (5.5 in, 5.5 out) while sitting on board. Reset.

The panic is not from lack of oxygen. It is from CO₂ sensitivity. You have trained for this.
