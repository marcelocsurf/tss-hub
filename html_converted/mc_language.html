TOOL 1 — POSTURE & BODY LANGUAGE

Expansive postures increase feelings of power. Contractive postures decrease them.

  • 33 independent experiments, 2,521 participants (Carney et al., 2015 review)
EXPANSIVE
  • Shoulders back, chest open
  • Head up, eye contact
  • Arms uncrossed, space taken
  • Weight distributed evenly
  • → Increased testosterone
  • → Decreased cortisol
  • → Greater risk tolerance
  • → Subjective feeling of power
CONTRACTIVE
  • Shoulders forward, hunched
  • Head down, avoiding gaze
  • Arms crossed, space minimized
  • Weight on one leg
  • → Decreased testosterone
  • → Increased cortisol
  • → Avoidance behavior
  • → Feeling of powerlessness
  • Furley & Schweizer (2014): Athletes who displayed positive body language after mistakes recovered faster and performed better on subsequent tasks.

In TSS: Power Posture (Block 4) is not just biomechanics — it’s a neurochemical command to your brain.

POSTURE EXERCISES FOR SURFERS

Pre-session power reset
  • Stand on the beach. Feet shoulder-width. Shoulders back. Chest open. Head up. Arms at sides or hands on hips. Hold 60 seconds. Breathe slowly. This is your starting state.
After a bad wave
  • Immediately: stand tall on your board. Shoulders back. Head up. Look at the horizon. DO NOT hunch, slump, or drop your head. Your body language determines the next wave.
Paddle posture awareness
  • While paddling: chest lifted, chin forward (not down), active scapula. This is both biomechanically better AND psychologically activating.
Beach briefing presence
  • When your coach speaks, stand expansive. When you speak, use open gestures. Body language affects how you receive AND deliver information.
The 3-second rule
  • After every wipeout: surface, take one breath, and within 3 seconds adopt full expansive posture on your board. The faster you reset physically, the faster you reset mentally.

TOOL 2 — SMILE & FACIAL FEEDBACK

Your face tells your brain how to feel. Not the other way around.

Facial Feedback Hypothesis

  • Confirmed in 19 countries, 3,878 participants (Coles et al., Nature Human Behaviour, 2022). Voluntary smiling amplifies and initiates feelings of happiness.

Running economy

  • Smiling while running improved movement economy vs. frowning. Lower perceived effort, reduced O₂ cost. Your face literally affects your efficiency (study cited by Fitzgerald, 2014).

Neurological mechanism

  • Smiling activates the zygomaticus major muscle, which sends feedback to the brain that modulates emotional processing. The brain interprets the smile as evidence of a positive state.

After mistakes

  • Athletes who smiled or maintained neutral expressions after errors recovered performance faster than those who showed frustration (Furley & Schweizer, 2014).

Contagion effect

  • Your smile affects your partner, your coach, your group. Mirror neurons cause others to unconsciously mimic your expression. One smile shifts the energy of a lineup.

TOOL 3 — SELF-TALK

  • Meta-analysis: self-talk interventions have a positive, moderate effect on sport performance (Hatzigeorgiadis et al., 2011).
INSTRUCTIONAL
  • Tells you WHAT to do.
  • “Low. Hold. Project.”
  • “Front foot. Look at target.”
  • “Commit. Don’t hesitate.”
  • Best for: technical execution,
  • new skills, precision tasks.
MOTIVATIONAL
  • Tells you HOW to feel.
  • “I’ve got this.”
  • “One more wave. I’m strong.”
  • “Trust the process.”
  • Best for: confidence, endurance,
  • pressure situations.

DESTRUCTIVE

  • “I always choke.” “I’m not good enough.”
  • “Why do I even try?” “Everyone is watching.”

CONSTRUCTIVE

  • “Next wave, fresh start.” “I trained for this.”
  • “Focus on process, not outcome.” “One thing at a time.”
You can’t stop the voice. But you CAN choose what it says.

TOOL 4 — COGNITIVE REFRAMING

Same event. Different interpretation. Different outcome.

“I feel nervous”

→

“I feel excited”
  • Same adrenaline. Different label. Research shows relabeling anxiety as excitement improves performance (Brooks, 2014).

“I fell again”

→

“I found the limit”
  • Reframing failure as data collection. The fall teaches you exactly where the edge is.

“The waves are bad”

→

“This is adaptive training”
  • Difficult conditions train reading, patience, and decision-making. Every session has value.

“I’m not improving”

→

“I’m in a consolidation phase”
  • Progress is not linear. Plateaus are where the brain integrates new patterns.

“They’re better than me”

→

“They show me what’s possible”
  • Comparison as aspiration, not diminishment. Watch, learn, then execute your own sequence.

TOOL 5 — ANCHORING

Create a trigger that instantly accesses your peak performance state.

1. RECALL
  • Close your eyes. Remember your BEST wave ever. See it. Feel the board. Hear the water. Feel the emotions: confidence, joy, power. Make it vivid.
2. INTENSIFY
  • Amplify the memory. Make it brighter, louder, closer. Feel it in your body. Where do you feel that confidence? Chest? Arms? Core?
3. ANCHOR
  • At the peak of the feeling, create a physical trigger: squeeze your fist, touch your thumb to your finger, or slap the board. Hold it for 5 seconds.
4. BREAK
  • Open your eyes. Shake it off. Think about something neutral for 10 seconds.
5. TEST
  • Fire the anchor: do the gesture. Does the feeling return? If yes — it’s set. If not — repeat steps 1–4 with more intensity. Build it daily.

Use your anchor before every session, before every heat, before every wave that matters.

TOOL 6 — PERCEPTUAL POSITIONS

See yourself from different angles to learn faster and regulate emotions.

1st PERSON
  • Your own eyes. Feel the wave, the board, the wind. Internal perspective. Best for: execution, anchoring, peak state recall.
  • “You feel the compression. You see the lip. You rotate.”
2nd PERSON
  • Your coach’s eyes. What does the coach see? Imagine their corrections. Best for: receiving feedback, understanding corrections.
  • “The coach sees your back knee. Your shoulders are too open.”
3rd PERSON
  • Neutral camera. Watch yourself from above or from the beach. No judgment. Best for: post-session review, learning from mistakes.
  • “The surfer hesitated on the takeoff. The angle was too shallow.”
