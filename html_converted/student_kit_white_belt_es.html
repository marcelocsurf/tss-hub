YOUR JOURNEY

▶

Where You Are

Welcome. You are about to learn to surf. Not through luck, not through chaos, and not by being thrown into the ocean hoping for the best. You are going to learn through a method — a clear, progressive, repeatable system that has trained beginners and Olympic athletes alike.

The White Belt is your foundation. Everything in surfing — every turn, every wave, every session for the rest of your life — depends on what you build here. There is no shortcut past this level. If you rush it, every belt after it will be weaker. If you build it right, everything accelerates naturally.

What You Will Learn

At White Belt, you will learn to control your surfboard before the ocean does. You will find your balance point on the board, align with wave energy, catch whitewater with intention, stand up with a clean technical pop-up, hold a stable power stance, control your speed, change direction, and exit safely.

You will do all of this in whitewater — the broken foam after a wave has collapsed. Whitewater is your training ground. It is predictable, manageable, and safe. You are not surfing real waves yet. You are building the control and awareness that makes real waves possible.

The Progression Map

You are here: White Belt — Beginner. Sequences #1 through #5.

After White Belt, you will move to Yellow Belt (Novato), where you will transition from whitewater to green (unbroken) waves. But first, everything starts here.

How to Use This Manual

This manual is your companion throughout the White Belt program. Each section covers one part of the surfing sequence. Scan the QR codes to watch video explanations of each step. Use the mission tracker to log your progress.

Read each section before your water session. Review it after. The goal is not to memorize — it is to understand the sequence so deeply that it becomes automatic.

SECTION 1: UNDERSTANDING YOUR ENVIRONMENT

▶

Before you touch the water, you need to understand where you are. The ocean is not a swimming pool. It has patterns, zones, and forces. At White Belt, you do not need to understand everything — but you need to understand enough to be safe and intentional.

What Is Whitewater?

Whitewater is the broken foam that travels toward shore after a wave has collapsed. It is white, bubbly, and relatively low-energy compared to the wave that created it. This is your training environment at White Belt.

Whitewater is predictable: it always moves toward shore, it always pushes you forward, and it always loses energy as it gets closer to the beach. This predictability is what makes it safe for learning.

Parts of the Beach

Shore break: Where waves or whitewater meet the sand. Can be surprisingly powerful. Enter carefully.

Shallow zone: Knee to waist-deep water. This is where most of your White Belt training happens.

Safe zone: The area defined by your coach before every session. You never leave it without permission.

Impact zone: Where waves are actively breaking. At White Belt, you stay away from this area.

Reading Conditions

Before every session, you will participate in a group venue analysis. Your coach will teach you to observe:

▸ Where the whitewater is coming from and in which direction it travels.

▸ How big the waves are and how frequently they arrive.

▸ Where other people are in the water.

▸ Where the safe zone is and where the hazards are (rocks, rip currents, shore break).

SECTION 2: SAFETY & ETIQUETTE

▶

Safety is not a topic at White Belt. It is the primary content. A beginner’s greatest risk is not wave size — it is lack of awareness. Every rule here exists because someone was hurt when it was ignored.

Communication Signals

These signals are taught on Day 1. Practice them until they require zero thinking.

Coach asks: ARE YOU OK? — You respond with the appropriate hand signal.

You are OK: Both arms form a triangle above your head.

You need help: One arm waving side to side above your head.

Coach signals COME IN: Cross both arms in an X above your head. Return to shore immediately.

Safety Rules

▸ Never place the surfboard between yourself and an incoming wave or whitewater.

▸ Never position yourself in front of another surfer’s board.

▸ When you fall: cover your head with both arms and fall away from the board. This is the star fall.

▸ Never grab the surfboard by the tail or leash when it is being pulled by a wave.

▸ If you lose your board: protect your head, surface calmly, retrieve the board without rushing toward other surfers.

▸ If in doubt, do not go out. If conditions feel beyond your level, tell your coach.

▸ Always carry the board at your side, not in front of you. Point the nose away from other people.

The Star Fall

The star fall is your non-negotiable exit at White Belt. When you fall or end your ride:

▸ Extend your arms and legs outward like a starfish.

▸ Fall backward, away from the board.

▸ Land flat on the water, distributing impact across your body.

Never jump off the board. Never dive headfirst. The star fall protects you in any depth.

Basic Etiquette

Even at White Belt, you share the water with others. Etiquette keeps everyone safe.

▸ Do not ditch your board: Always maintain control. A loose board is a danger to everyone.

▸ Be aware of your surroundings: Before you catch a wave, look behind you and beside you.

▸ Stay in the safe zone: Do not drift outside the area your coach defined.

▸ Respect spacing: Do not crowd other surfers. Give everyone room.

Conditions for White Belt

Maximum wave height: 1–2 feet (whitewater only). No unbroken waves. No overhead conditions. No strong currents.

If conditions exceed this limit, the session moves to land drills or theory review.

Equipment: Soft-top surfboard. Leash checked every session. Appropriate rash guard or wetsuit.

Supervision: Maximum 1 coach per 4–6 students in water. Coach must be in the water during the session.

SECTION 3: THE SEQUENCE

▶

This is the heart of The Surf Sequence. Every surf session follows this order. At White Belt, you are performing this sequence for the first time — entirely in whitewater.

Each step builds on the previous one. Skip a step and the sequence breaks. Master each step and the next one becomes easier.

6

BLOCK 1 — PREPARATION

▶

Purpose: Create awareness, safety, and intention before entering the water.

7

STEP 01 GOOFY OR REGULAR

▶

Before anything else, identify your stance. Goofy means left foot back. Regular means right foot back. Your coach will help you test which feels natural. Once you know your stance, use it consistently.

8

STEP 02 VENUE ANALYSIS

▶

Before touching the water, read the beach. Where is the whitewater coming from? How big is it? Where are the other people? Where is the safe zone? Your coach will lead this analysis, but you participate actively.

9

STEP 03 SAFETY RULES

▶

Review the safety rules before every session. Not as a formality — as a real check. Is the leash attached? Do you remember the signals? Do you know the safe zone?

10

STEP 04 WARM-UP

▶

Activate your body before entering the water. Mobility, body awareness, and mental focus. The warm-up is not just physical — it is the transition from daily life into surf mode.

11

BLOCK 2 — ENTRY & BOARD CONTROL

▶

Purpose: Enter the ocean safely and maintain control of your board at all times.

12

STEP 05 GRAB BOARD

▶

Handle your board with intention. Back hand on the tail, front hand near the center. Carry it at your side, not in front of you. The nose points away from people. The board is your tool — control starts before you enter the ocean.

13

STEP 06 WALK OUT

▶

Navigate to the water safely. Be aware of the shore break, rocks, and other people. Keep the board at your side. Walk until the water is knee to waist deep. Face the incoming whitewater.

14

STEP 07 PUT BOARD IN THE WATER

▶

Place the board in the water with control. The nose faces the incoming whitewater. You hold the board — the board does not drift away from you.

15

STEP 08 CONTROL YOUR BOARD

▶

Manage your board through incoming whitewater. Use your hands to steer it. When whitewater approaches, tilt the nose slightly up so the foam passes underneath. The board responds to intentional input — not to panic. This is the first lesson: the board is an extension of your body.

16

STEP 09 GO THROUGH WHITEWATER STANDING

▶

Stay standing while whitewater pushes past you. Keep control of the board. Do not let the foam knock you over or rip the board away.

17

STEP 10 TURN AROUND SAFELY

▶

Turn your body and board to face the shore. Use the pivot concept: press the tail with your back hand to lift the nose, then swing the board around. Turn safely without hitting anyone.

18

STEP 11 WALK BACK TO THE SAND

▶

Exit the water calmly. Carry the board correctly. Reset for the next attempt.

19

BLOCK 3 — CATCHING WAVES PRONE

▶

Purpose: Connect with wave energy while lying on the board and learn to ride before standing.

20

STEP 12 GET ON YOUR BOARD — FIND SWEET SPOT

▶

Climb onto the board and find your sweet spot. This is the position where the board floats level — the nose is not diving, not popping up. Too far forward: the nose sinks. Too far back: you drag through the water. The sweet spot is the position of maximum efficiency.

21

STEP 13 GET ALIGNED WITH THE WHITEWATER

▶

Before you paddle, make sure your board is pointing straight toward shore and aligned with the incoming whitewater. If the board is angled or crooked, the wave energy will push you sideways. Alignment determines success before the wave arrives.

22

STEP 14 PADDLE TO CATCH WHITEWATER

▶

When whitewater is approaching, paddle with continuous, committed strokes. Long, deep strokes. Elbows high. Push water behind you, not to the side. The goal is to match the speed of the whitewater so it catches you and carries you forward.

23

STEP 15 COBRA — PICK YOUR LINE

▶

Once the whitewater catches you, immediately go into cobra position: push your chest up with your hands, eyes forward, scanning. This is where you make your first decision: will you go left or right? Look in the direction you want to go. The cobra is not just a position — it is the moment you choose your line.

24

STEP 16 DISMOUNT

▶

At this stage, you exit the board safely after riding prone. Let the whitewater carry you, then slide off the board to the side, protecting your head.

25

BLOCK 4 — STANDING UP

▶

Purpose: Transition from prone to standing and establish stability on a moving board.

26

STEP 17 POP-UP + FEET POSITION

▶

This is the moment you stand up on the surfboard. The pop-up starts from a good cobra position with hands at rib height. Drag your back leg forward into a figure-four position, load weight onto your back foot, bring your front foot between your hands near the center of the board. Hips lower before hands release. This is not a jump — it is a controlled, technical movement.

27

STEP 18 POSTURE — POWER STANCE

▶

Once standing, establish your power stance. Knees bent, back straight, chest open, eyes forward, arms open for balance, weight centered. This is the foundation of all power transfer in surfing. If your posture is good, everything else gets easier.

28

STEP 19 IMPULSE

▶

Impulse is the technique for maintaining or recovering speed on the wave. At White Belt, it is introduced as a concept and first practice. A coordinated knee flexion combined with a double-arm push that creates forward momentum. When you feel the board slowing down, impulse keeps you moving.

29

STEP 20 STARFISH DISMOUNT

▶

The controlled exit from every wave. When your ride is ending, extend your arms and legs like a starfish, fall backward away from the board. Do not jump off. Do not dive. At White Belt, this is non-negotiable. Every ride ends with a starfish.

30

BLOCK 5 — DIRECTIONAL CONTROL & PADDLE-OUT READINESS

▶

Purpose: Add intentional direction changes and prepare for independent paddle-out.

31

STEP 21 SET GOAL

▶

Every session needs a clear mission. Not ‘have fun’ — a specific, measurable objective. Example: ‘Catch 5 whitewater waves with a clean pop-up.’ Your coach will help you set this, but you should understand why you are in the water.

32

STEP 22 TURN BACKSIDE

▶

Initiate a small directional change to your backside. The rotation starts from your eyes: look where you want to go. Your head follows your eyes, your torso follows your head, your hips follow your torso, and the board follows your body. At White Belt, this is a small direction change in whitewater — not a full turn.

33

STEP 23 TURN FRONTSIDE

▶

Same as the backside turn, but in the opposite direction — toward the side your chest faces. Eyes lead, body follows, board responds.

34

STEP 24 PADDLE OUT

▶

Paddle from shore toward the lineup. At White Belt, this means paddling through incoming whitewater with continuous effort. Centered position on the board, long strokes, and the determination to keep going.

35

STEP 25 TURTLE ROLL

▶

When a wave of whitewater is about to hit you while paddling out, the turtle roll lets you pass through it. Grip the rails, flip the board upside down over you, hold tight, let the whitewater pass, then roll back. At White Belt, this is introduced. Mastery comes at Yellow Belt.

36

STEP 26 TURN LEFT AND RIGHT LYING ON BOARD

▶

While lying on the board, use your hands and weight to adjust your direction. This helps you reposition between waves without having to stand up or start over.

37

SECTION 4: THE INFINITE CIRCLE

▶

Here is the secret that will change how you see surfing: the sequence you just learned repeats. It repeats at every level. It repeats on every wave. It repeats for the rest of your surfing life.

At White Belt, you experience the full circle for the first time: you prepare, you enter, you catch, you stand, you ride, you exit, you reset. Then you do it again.

What changes at each level is not the structure — it is the depth. At White Belt, most of your attention goes to the early steps: controlling the board, finding the sweet spot, aligning. The later steps — turning, speed control — are present but receive less attention.

At Yellow Belt, the same circle operates on green waves. The early steps become more automatic, and your attention shifts to the middle and later steps.

SECTION 5: YOUR MISSIONS

▶

Missions are your in-water practice assignments. Each mission has a specific objective. Focus on one mission per session or per set of waves. Record your results using the tracker.

MISSION 1 Board Control

▸ Carry the board safely to the water using correct hand placement

▸ Control the board through incoming whitewater using your hands

▸ Tilt the nose up to let whitewater pass underneath

▸ Perform a safe pivot turn to change direction

▸ Pass through whitewater standing without losing the board

MISSION 2 Sweet Spot + Alignment + Wave Catch

▸ Get on the board and find the sweet spot within 1–2 attempts

▸ Align the nose with incoming whitewater before paddling

▸ Paddle with continuous, committed strokes until the wave catches you

▸ Go into cobra position immediately after catching

▸ Choose left or right in cobra

▸ Controlled dismount (slide off or starfish)

MISSION 3 Technical Pop-Up + Power Stance

▸ Execute a clean, controlled pop-up from cobra position

▸ Front foot lands near the center of the board

▸ Hips lower before hands release

▸ Establish a recognizable power stance: knees bent, chest open, eyes forward

▸ Hold the stance for 3+ seconds

▸ Starfish dismount after riding

MISSION 4 Front Foot Placement

▸ Precise front foot landing in the center of the board during pop-up

▸ Use the Two-Second Pop-Up: keep at least one point of contact until foot position is confirmed

▸ Hands stay on the board until the foot is correctly placed

▸ Repeat across multiple waves with improving consistency

MISSION 5 Brake & Accelerator

▸ Move weight forward on the board while standing (accelerator)

▸ Move weight backward on the board while standing (brake)

▸ Feel the speed change when shifting weight intentionally

▸ Attempt impulse: coordinated knee flexion + arm push to maintain speed

MISSION 6 Basic Direction Change

▸ Catch a whitewater wave and pop up

▸ Initiate a small frontside direction change using the kinetic chain (eyes → neck → torso → hips)

▸ Initiate a small backside direction change

▸ Return to power stance after each direction change

▸ Starfish dismount

MISSION 7 Paddle-Out + Turtle Roll

▸ Paddle out through incoming whitewater with continuous effort

▸ Perform a basic turtle roll when whitewater approaches

▸ Recover to prone position after the turtle roll

▸ Adjust board direction while lying down using hand and weight shifts

▸ Complete a full cycle: paddle out, reposition, catch, ride, exit

SECTION 6: ON-LAND DRILLS

▶

On-land drills prepare your nervous system, simplify technique, reduce errors in the water, and accelerate real learning. Practice these before every session and between sessions at home.

DRILL L1 Pop-Up + Power Stance (Flat Surface)

Objective: Learn a clean, technical pop-up and immediately connect it to a stable power stance.

1. Lie flat on the board (or marked area). Hands at rib height.

2. Cobra position: chest up, eyes forward.

3. Drag back leg into figure-four. Load weight on back foot.

4. Bring front foot between hands, near center of board.

5. Hips lower. Hands release. Stand into power stance.

6. Hold for 2 seconds. Eyes forward, back straight, knees bent.

7. Repeat 10 times. Focus on precision, not speed.

DRILL L2 Sweet Spot Discovery

Objective: Develop awareness of the correct body position on the board.

1. Place the board on flat ground or soft sand.

2. Lie on the board in different positions: too far forward, too far back, then centered.

3. Feel the difference: nose-heavy, tail-heavy, balanced.

4. Have a partner gently push the board from behind. Notice which position moves most efficiently.

5. Mark your sweet spot position mentally. Practice finding it in under 2 seconds.

6. Repeat 5 times. Close your eyes on the last attempt.

DRILL L3 Board Handling Circuit

Objective: Build confidence and control carrying, placing, and managing the board on land.

1. Pick up the board using correct hand placement (back hand on tail, front hand center).

2. Walk 20 meters carrying it at your side, nose pointing away from people.

3. Place the board on the ground gently, fins up.

4. Pick it up again. Perform a pivot turn: press the tail, swing the nose 180 degrees.

5. Repeat the full circuit 3 times. Each time, increase speed slightly.

6. Coach evaluates: control, awareness of surroundings, correct hand placement.

DRILL L4 Cobra + Direction Choice

Objective: Train the transition from catching to deciding direction.

1. Lie on the board in prone position. Hands at rib height.

2. Coach calls a whitewater wave (imaginary). Paddle 5 strokes.

3. Go into cobra position: chest up, eyes scanning.

4. Coach calls ‘Left!’ or ‘Right!’ — look in that direction immediately.

5. Hold cobra with eyes locked on your chosen line for 2 seconds.

6. Return to prone. Repeat 10 times, alternating directions.

DRILL L5 Star Fall Practice

Objective: Make the star fall automatic and remove fear of falling.

1. Stand on the board on soft sand or grass.

2. On the coach’s signal, perform a star fall: extend arms and legs, fall backward.

3. Land flat, away from the board. Cover your head during the fall.

4. Stand up. Return to the board. Repeat.

5. 5 repetitions with eyes open. 3 repetitions with eyes closed.

6. Coach evaluates: controlled extension, backward fall, head protection.

DRILL L6 Kinetic Chain Rotation

Objective: Introduce rotation as a result of vision and body chain activation.

1. Stand in power stance on the board.

2. Look to your frontside. Let your neck rotate, then torso, then hips.

3. Feel the board respond to the chain: eyes → neck → torso → hips → ankles.

4. Return to center. Repeat backside.

5. Do 5 frontside and 5 backside rotations. Start slow, build smoothness.

6. Coach evaluates: sequence of rotation (eyes first), posture maintenance, board response.

SECTION 7: KEY CONCEPTS REFERENCE

▶

These are the mental models that drive your surfing at White Belt. Return to this page whenever you need a reminder.

The Board Is Your Tool

The surfboard is not something that drags you around. It is something you control. Every interaction with the board should be intentional. When you treat the board as an extension of your body, everything changes.

Alignment Before Action

Nothing happens until the board is aligned with wave energy. Alignment determines success before the wave arrives. This principle applies at every level of surfing.

Stability Before Speed

You must be stable before adding complexity. A clean pop-up builds confidence automatically. A rushed pop-up builds fear. At White Belt, control matters more than anything else.

The Sequence Is the Method

Every surf session follows the same structure. What changes is what you focus on inside the sequence. The structure never changes. This creates predictability, reduces anxiety, and makes progress measurable.

Control Before Performance

At White Belt, you are not measured by tricks, wave count, or style. You are measured by how much control and awareness you demonstrate.

P·R·C·H — The Four Basic Movements

Every movement in surfing can be understood through four patterns:

P — Posture: The foundation of all power transfer. Back straight, head up, aligned with center of board.

R — Rotation: The engine of every turn. Eyes lead, body follows, energy transfers to feet and board.

C — Compression: Creates internal energy. Lower your center of gravity. Compress then extend for speed.

H — Hold: Sustain the position longer than feels natural. Hold through the apex, then release.

At White Belt, Posture is your primary focus. Rotation is introduced through basic direction changes. Compression appears in your pop-up and stance. Hold will develop at higher levels.

Three Circles of Power

Your surfing depends on three elements working together:

Circle 1 — Body: Your posture, rotation, compression, and hold. How you move.

Circle 2 — Board: Your foot position and weight distribution. How the board responds.

Circle 3 — Wave: The energy, direction, and structure of the wave. What the ocean gives you.

At White Belt, you are learning each circle as a separate concept. When all three align, you experience your first real ride.

Feet Position on the Board

Sweet Spot (Prone): The position where the board floats level. Not nose-heavy, not tail-heavy.

Center (Standing): Front foot in the center of the board on the stringer. This is your axis of rotation.

Brake: Weight shifts toward the tail. The board slows down.

Accelerator: Weight shifts toward the nose. The board speeds up.

SECTION 8: YOUR PROGRESSION

▶

Progression at White Belt is not measured in days. It is measured in observable behaviors across multiple sessions. Here is what advancement looks like.

Early White Belt (Sequences #1–#2)

▸ You pick up and carry the board correctly without verbal cues.

▸ You enter the water facing waves and maintain board control.

▸ You find the sweet spot on the first or second attempt.

▸ You align with whitewater before paddling — not after.

▸ You catch whitewater consistently (more catches than misses).

Mid White Belt (Sequences #3–#4)

▸ You hold cobra position and make a directional choice before popping up.

▸ Your pop-up is smooth enough that you maintain balance for 3+ seconds.

▸ Your front foot lands near the center of the board without major correction.

▸ You apply brake or accelerator intentionally at least once per wave.

▸ You perform a star fall consistently — no jumping off the board.

▸ You initiate basic direction changes (frontside and backside) in whitewater.

Late White Belt (Sequence #5 / Approaching Promotion)

▸ You paddle out through whitewater with continuous effort.

▸ You attempt a basic turtle roll when whitewater approaches.

▸ You choose direction intentionally before popping up.

▸ You manage the full cycle independently: enter, catch, ride, exit, reset.

▸ You participate actively in venue analysis and can identify the safe zone.

▸ You show reduced frustration over consecutive sessions — you accept falls and reset calmly.

▸ You can describe in your own words what the mission of the session was.

What Comes Next: Yellow Belt (Novato)

At Yellow Belt, you move from whitewater to green (unbroken) waves. The entire sequence repeats, but the environment changes everything. You will learn to read the wave before it breaks, choose where to position yourself, manage paddling speeds, angle your takeoff, and find the pocket — the power source of the wave.

But that only happens if your White Belt is solid. Everything you build here is the foundation Yellow Belt demands.
